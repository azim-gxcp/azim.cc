## Option A: Pedagogical Innovation (Workshop, 60 min)

### Title
**Rethinking How We Teach Money: A Hands-On Workshop on Fixed-Supply Monetary Architecture and Its Implications for K-12 Economics Education**

### Abstract

What if the way we teach money is built on assumptions that are not laws of nature but design choices? Students across the Muslim world learn that inflation is inevitable, that interest is the time-value of money, and that central banks must expand the money supply to sustain growth. These are presented as economic facts. They are, in reality, features of one particular monetary architecture, one that the Quran's prohibition of Riba and the classical scholars' insistence on honest measure directly challenge.

This workshop introduces educators to a fundamentally different monetary architecture: fixed-supply, interest-free, identity-based, and built on the principles the Islamic tradition has always articulated but never had the technology to implement at scale. Participants will interact with a live wallet system, execute zero-fee cross-border transfers, and observe how a velocity mechanism replaces inflation and taxation simultaneously, all in real time on their own devices.

The session is structured in three parts. The first examines what we currently teach about money and where those assumptions originate. The second provides a hands-on demonstration of an alternative architecture, allowing participants to experience fixed supply, grant-based distribution, and profit-sharing lending as operational realities rather than theoretical concepts. The third explores what changes in K-12 economics, finance, and financial literacy curricula when students learn that monetary systems are designed, not discovered.

This is not a technology demonstration. It is a pedagogical inquiry into whether the Ummah's educators are preparing students for a monetary future that is already being built, or for a monetary past that is beginning to fracture.

**Keywords:** monetary architecture, Islamic economics education, financial literacy, fixed-supply currency, K-12 curriculum, pedagogical innovation, tajdid

## Option B: Purpose and Worldview (Practice-Led Inquiry, 60 min)

### Title
**Money as a Trust: Reconnecting Islamic First Principles with What We Teach Students About Wealth, Exchange, and Economic Life**

### Abstract

The Islamic tradition is unambiguous: wealth is a trust (Amanah), not a possession. The Quran states it directly: "Spend out of that in which He has made you trustees" (57:7). Yet the economics and financial literacy we deliver in our schools is built entirely on the framework of ownership, interest, and state-issued fiat currency, a framework that conflicts with these principles at its foundation.

This practice-led inquiry asks a question most Islamic schools have not yet confronted: are we teaching our students a theory of money that contradicts what we teach them about Allah's sovereignty over wealth? When we explain inflation as natural, interest as the cost of capital, and debt as a necessary instrument of growth, are we presenting facts, or are we presenting the assumptions of one specific system as universal truths?

The session presents primary-source evidence from the Quran and Sunnah alongside a working alternative: a monetary system with fixed supply (no inflation by design), zero interest (profit-sharing replaces it), and grant-based distribution (wealth enters circulation through identity, not through debt). Participants will examine the Arabic roots of Riba, Qard, Dayn, and Bay' to understand what these terms actually mean before their institutional reinterpretation, and then interact with a live system that operationalises these principles.

The inquiry concludes with a practical framework for school leaders: what would a K-12 economics and financial literacy curriculum look like if it began from Islamic first principles rather than retrofitting Shariah compliance onto a conventional syllabus?

**Keywords:** Amanah, wealth as trust, Islamic worldview, economics curriculum, Riba, first principles, financial literacy, Shariah-first education, tajdid

## Option C: Emerging Ideas / Leadership (Workshop, 60 min)

### Title
**Preparing Students for a Post-Fiat World: What School Leaders Need to Know About the Monetary Transition Already Underway**

### Abstract

A structural shift in global monetary architecture is underway. Central Bank Digital Currencies are being piloted by over 130 countries. Tokenised assets, blockchain-based settlement, and alternative monetary protocols are moving from theory to deployment. Within the next decade, the monetary system your students will operate in may look fundamentally different from the one you were trained to teach.

Most Islamic schools are not preparing for this. Economics curricula still centre on fractional reserve banking, central bank interest rate policy, and the assumption that fiat currency is the permanent medium of exchange. These are not wrong as descriptions of the current system, but they are incomplete as preparation for what comes next.

This workshop equips school leaders and curriculum designers with a practical understanding of what is changing and what it means for their institutions. Participants will interact with a live monetary protocol: creating wallets on their devices, sending cross-border transactions at near-zero cost, and observing mechanisms (velocity-based circulation incentives, interest-free lending pools, identity-based account recovery) that do not exist in the conventional system they currently teach.

The session is structured around three questions every school leader must answer. First: do your students understand that monetary systems are designed, and that the current design is one of several possibilities? Second: does your financial literacy programme prepare students for a world where money may not be issued by governments, where cross-border transfer may cost nothing, and where interest may not exist? Third: what does your school need to change in the next three years to remain relevant?

The workshop does not advocate for any single system. It demonstrates that alternatives exist, that they are operational, and that educators who understand them will lead institutions that prepare students for the world as it is becoming, not the world as it was.

**Keywords:** monetary transition, CBDC, blockchain, school leadership, future-ready curriculum, financial literacy, digital assets, K-12 strategy, emerging technology in education

## Recommendation

**Option A** is the safest and most directly useful for the GAIS audience. It is hands-on, practical, and delivers something educators can take back to their classrooms.

**Option B** is the most intellectually compelling and the most aligned with the conference theme of Tajdid. It challenges the audience at the worldview level. Higher risk, higher impact. Best if the audience is ready for deep inquiry.

**Option C** is the best for an audience of school leaders and decision-makers who need strategic framing more than pedagogical detail. It is the least confrontational and the easiest to accept.

All three include the hands-on wallet demonstration. The difference is framing: pedagogy, worldview, or strategy.
