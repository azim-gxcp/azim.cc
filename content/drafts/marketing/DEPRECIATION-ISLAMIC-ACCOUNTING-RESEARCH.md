## 1. The Question

A business purchases a delivery vehicle for USD 25,000. The cash leaves the account today. The vehicle is parked in the lot today. The transaction is complete today.

Under IFRS (IAS 16) and GAAP (ASC 360), this is not treated as a USD 25,000 expense. Instead, the business is instructed to record a USD 25,000 "asset" on the balance sheet and then spread the cost across 5 years as a USD 5,000 annual "depreciation expense."

The question: **Why are we lying to our own books?**

The cash is gone. The full amount has been paid. The transaction is settled. Yet the financial statements declare that only USD 5,000 was spent this year, and the remaining USD 20,000 is somehow still "held" as an asset whose value will be consumed over the next four years.

This is the question that conventional accounting does not answer honestly. And this is the question that Islamic accounting professionals have failed to ask.

## 2. Conventional Depreciation — The Standard Explanation

### What Is Depreciation?

Depreciation is an accounting method that allocates the cost of a tangible fixed asset over its estimated useful life. The underlying theory is the **matching principle** — expenses should be recognised in the same period as the revenues they help generate.

**Key standards governing depreciation:**

| Standard | Framework | Reference |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| IAS 16 — Property, Plant and Equipment | IFRS (International) | Paragraphs 43-62 |
| ASC 360 — Property, Plant and Equipment | US GAAP | Subtopic 360-10 |
| FAS 13 — Property, Plant and Equipment | AAOIFI (Islamic) | Sections on recognition and measurement |

### Common Depreciation Methods

| Method | Calculation | Use Case |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Straight-Line** | (Cost - Residual Value) / Useful Life | Most common; equal annual expense |
| **Declining Balance** | Net Book Value × Fixed Rate | Higher expense in early years |
| **Units of Production** | (Cost - Residual) / Total Units × Units Produced | Manufacturing equipment |
| **Sum-of-Years' Digits** | Weighted declining allocation | Accelerated recognition |

### What Depreciation Does NOT Apply To

Depreciation applies to tangible fixed assets with a finite useful life. It does **not** apply to:

- **Land** — considered to have an indefinite useful life (IAS 16.58)
- **Investment property measured at fair value** (IAS 40)
- **Biological assets** measured at fair value less costs to sell (IAS 41)
- **Mineral rights and similar resources** (covered by IFRS 6)

Buildings, however, ARE depreciated under both IFRS and GAAP, even though the land beneath them is not. This distinction itself reveals an internal contradiction — the building sits on land that appreciates while the building itself is presumed to lose value on a schedule.

### The Stated Rationale

The accounting profession offers three justifications for depreciation:

1. **Matching Principle:** The cost of an asset should be matched against the revenue it generates over its useful life, not charged entirely in the year of purchase.

2. **True and Fair View:** Expensing a large asset purchase immediately would distort the income statement, showing an artificially low profit (or a loss) in the year of purchase and artificially high profits in subsequent years.

3. **Asset Valuation:** The balance sheet should reflect the declining economic value of the asset as it ages, wears out, or becomes obsolete.

Each of these rationales collapses under scrutiny when examined through the lens of Islamic first principles.

## 3. How Depreciation Works in Practice

### Example: Vehicle Purchase

A business purchases a delivery vehicle for USD 25,000 with an estimated useful life of 5 years and no residual value. Under straight-line depreciation:

**Year of Purchase — What Actually Happened:**
- Cash paid: USD 25,000 (gone from the bank account)
- Vehicle received: physically present, fully owned

**Year of Purchase — What the Books Say:**
- Expense recorded: USD 5,000 (only 20% of the actual cash spent)
- Asset on balance sheet: USD 25,000 (as if the money is still held in some form)

**Over 5 Years — What the Books Continue to Say:**

| Year | Depreciation Expense | Net Book Value | Cash Actually Remaining |
|------|---------------------|----------------|------------------------|
| 1 | USD 5,000 | USD 20,000 | USD 0 (paid in full) |
| 2 | USD 5,000 | USD 15,000 | USD 0 |
| 3 | USD 5,000 | USD 10,000 | USD 0 |
| 4 | USD 5,000 | USD 5,000 | USD 0 |
| 5 | USD 5,000 | USD 0 | USD 0 |

The cash column is the truth. The depreciation column is the fiction. For five years, the financial statements report expenses that do not correspond to any cash movement, and they carry an "asset" value that does not correspond to any recoverable cash.

### The Balance Sheet Distortion

After Year 1, the balance sheet shows a USD 20,000 asset. This number represents neither the cash paid (which was USD 25,000, already gone) nor the market value of the vehicle (which depreciates at an unpredictable rate, not a straight line). It represents a mathematical abstraction — the portion of the original cost that the accounting standard has decided to defer.

An investor, a creditor, a beneficiary, or a stakeholder reading this balance sheet is led to believe the business holds USD 20,000 in asset value. In reality, the business holds a used vehicle whose actual market value could be anywhere from USD 8,000 to USD 18,000, and the USD 25,000 cash is irrecoverably spent.

This is not a "true and fair view." This is a structured misrepresentation.

## 4. The Islamic Framework — First Principles

Islamic economic principles do not exist in isolation from accounting. The Quran and the Sunnah establish clear directives about how financial transactions must be recorded, and these directives carry specific implications for the treatment of asset purchases.

### 4.1 The Commandment to Record Truthfully (Al-Sidq — الصِّدْق)

> **"O you who believe, fear Allah and be with the truthful."**
> — Quran 9:119 (Surah Al-Tawbah)

> **"O you who believe, when you contract a debt for a specified term, write it down. And let a scribe write between you in justice."**
> — Quran 2:282 (Ayat al-Dayn — آيَة الدَّيْن)

Ayat al-Dayn is the longest verse in the Quran. It is devoted entirely to the proper recording of financial transactions. The instruction is unambiguous: **write it down, and write it in justice.**

The operative word is **bil-adl** (بِالْعَدْلِ) — "in justice," "in fairness," "in truth." The accountant who prepares financial statements is a **katib** (كَاتِب — scribe/recorder). The financial statement itself is a form of **shahadah** (شَهَادَة — testimony/witness). A financial record that reports USD 5,000 spent when USD 25,000 was actually paid does not meet the standard of bil-adl. The record must reflect what happened, not what an accounting theory assumes will happen over the next five years.

> **"Woe to those who give short measure — who, when they take a measure from people, take in full, but if they give by measure or weight to others, give less than due."**
> — Quran 83:1-3 (Surah Al-Mutaffifin)

A financial statement that shows USD 40,000 in "asset value" when the cash is gone is giving a measurement that does not correspond to reality. The stakeholder receives an inflated picture. The institution receives the benefit of appearing more solvent than it is. This is precisely the asymmetry Al-Mutaffifin warns against.

### 4.2 The Spot Transaction Principle (Bay' al-Naqd — بَيْع نَقْد)

When a business pays USD 25,000 for a vehicle, this is a **bay' al-naqd** (بَيْع نَقْد — spot sale). Both the item and the price were exchanged immediately, hand to hand. No **dayn** (دَيْن — debt/obligation) was created. The transaction is complete at the point of exchange. This is the cleanest form of **bay'** (بَيْع — trade) in Islamic jurisprudence.

Depreciation takes this completed, settled, hand-to-hand transaction and artificially extends it across time in the books. It treats a fully paid expense as though it were being incurred gradually. There is no Quranic, Hadith, or jurisprudential basis for treating a completed transaction as ongoing. The transaction is done. The expense is real. The cash is gone.

### 4.3 The Prohibition of Gharar (غَرَر — Uncertainty)

> **"The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) forbade the sale involving gharar."**
> — Sahih Muslim, Book 21, Hadith 10

Gharar is uncertainty, ambiguity, or speculation in a transaction. It is prohibited in Islamic commerce because it introduces an element of deception — one party knows less than they should about the true nature of the exchange.

Depreciation introduces gharar into financial statements. The "useful life" of an asset is an estimate. The residual value is a guess. The depreciation schedule is a projection. Every number on the balance sheet related to a depreciated asset is, by definition, uncertain. A stakeholder reading the financial statements is making decisions based on projections masquerading as facts.

### 4.4 The Principle of Immediate Settlement (Yadan bi Yad — يَدًا بِيَدٍ)

> **"Gold for gold, silver for silver, wheat for wheat, barley for barley, dates for dates, salt for salt — like for like, equal for equal, hand to hand."**
> — Sahih Muslim, Book 22, Hadith 75

This hadith establishes the principle of immediate, complete settlement in exchange. The phrase **yadan bi yad** (يَدًا بِيَدٍ) — "hand to hand" — requires that transactions be completed in the present, not deferred into the future.

Depreciation violates this principle. The transaction is complete — the asset is received, the cash is paid — but the accounting treatment defers the recognition of the expense across multiple future periods. The books do not reflect the completed transaction. They reflect a deferred, artificial allocation that stretches the reality of a single settled exchange across years.

### 4.5 The Phantom Debt (Dayn That Is Not a Dayn)

The longest ayah in the Quran (Al-Baqarah 2:282) establishes a comprehensive framework for recording financial obligations. Its core principles: write it down, be specific, be just, have witnesses, and do not alter the record. The entire system is designed to ensure that financial records reflect reality.

Depreciation does something the ayah never contemplates: it records an expense that has already been fully paid as though it were still being incurred. There is no **dayn** (دَيْن) outstanding. There is no creditor waiting. There is no obligation unfulfilled. Yet the books carry a "balance" forward year after year as though something remains to be settled. This is not recording a debt. It is manufacturing a phantom liability where none exists.

## 5. The Hand-to-Hand Principle Applied to Accounting

The hadith on hand-to-hand exchange is typically applied to trade between parties. The argument presented here extends it to a principle that also governs how an entity records its own transactions — because the purpose of the principle is truthful representation of economic reality.

### The Transaction Is Complete

When a business pays USD 25,000 for a vehicle:

- The seller has received the full payment
- The buyer has received the full asset
- No debt exists between the parties
- No future obligation remains

The exchange is **settled**. Hand to hand. Complete.

Yet depreciation forces the buyer's accounting records to behave as if the transaction is still in progress — as if the expense is being "consumed" gradually over five years. This contradicts the settled nature of the transaction.

### Internal Honesty

The hand-to-hand principle is not merely about transactions between two parties. It reflects a broader Islamic value: **truthfulness in all dealings, including with oneself.** A business that records a USD 25,000 cash outflow as a USD 5,000 expense is not lying to a counterparty — it is lying to its own books. And the books exist to inform decisions by the owner, the management, the investors, and the beneficiaries.

The Islamic standard demands that the books reflect reality. The reality is: USD 25,000 was spent. The honest record is: USD 25,000 in expense.

## 6. Gharar in Financial Statements

### The Assumptions Behind Depreciation

Every depreciation calculation requires the following estimates:

| Estimate | Certainty Level | Islamic Classification |
|----------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Useful life** (e.g., 5 years) | Uncertain — vehicle could last 3 years or 10 | Gharar |
| **Residual value** (e.g., USD 0 or USD 3,000) | Uncertain — depends on market conditions at disposal | Gharar |
| **Depreciation method** (straight-line vs. declining) | Arbitrary choice — affects reported profit | Gharar |
| **Impairment triggers** | Subjective assessment — management discretion | Gharar |

None of these numbers are facts. They are projections. Yet they determine:

- How much profit the business reports each year
- How much tax the business pays
- What asset value stakeholders rely upon for decisions
- Whether the business appears solvent or distressed

Building a financial reporting framework on projections, and presenting those projections as factual statements of position, embeds gharar into the very fabric of financial reporting.

### The Unforeseen Event

The conventional argument states that depreciation provides a more accurate picture by spreading cost over the period of benefit. Consider what happens when reality departs from the estimate:

- **Year 1:** The delivery vehicle is involved in an accident and is written off. The books show a USD 20,000 "asset" that no longer exists. An impairment write-down is required — an admission that the original depreciation schedule was wrong from the start.

- **Year 1:** A new building, inaugurated months ago, is destroyed by an earthquake, flood, or fire. The balance sheet shows millions in "asset value" that has been reduced to rubble.

In both cases, the depreciation schedule provided a false sense of security. The books showed value that did not exist. Under immediate expensing, the financial statements would have reflected reality: the full cost was borne when paid, and the loss event is simply a loss of the physical asset, not a balance sheet shock.

The argument that immediate expensing creates "sudden high expenses" is mathematically inaccurate. If the expense is high in Year 1, the income statement absorbs it in Year 1. Over the 5-year period, the total expense is identical under both methods. The only difference is timing — and the depreciation method's timing is based on assumptions that reality routinely contradicts.

## 7. The Illusion of Depreciation — Economic Reality

### The Replacement Trap

Depreciation creates a dangerous illusion: the belief that the "annual depreciation expense" somehow represents the cost of using the asset each year. It does not. It represents an arbitrary fraction of the original purchase price.

At the end of the 5-year depreciation schedule, the business must replace the vehicle. The replacement cost is not USD 25,000 — it is whatever the market price is at that time, likely higher due to inflation. The business has been recording USD 5,000 per year as the "cost" of the vehicle, but it has not set aside USD 5,000 per year for replacement. The depreciation expense exists only on paper. No cash has been reserved.

The business now faces a dilemma: it needs a new vehicle, the old one is fully depreciated (book value: zero), and the cash was spent five years ago. The depreciation schedule gave the illusion that the cost was being managed gradually, when in fact, the full economic burden was borne on Day 1 and the replacement burden lands in full on Day 1,826.

### Immediate Expensing Reflects the Truth

Under immediate expensing:

- **Year 1:** USD 25,000 expense recorded. The income statement shows the full impact. The business and its stakeholders know exactly where they stand.
- **Years 2-5:** No phantom expense. No artificial reduction in profit. No "book value" to mislead.
- **Year 6 (replacement):** Another full expense recorded. Again, transparent and immediate.

At no point does the financial statement contain a number that represents something other than what actually happened. No estimate. No projection. No gharar.

### The "Distortion" Argument Reversed

Conventional accounting argues that immediate expensing "distorts" the income statement by showing an unusually low profit (or a loss) in the year of purchase. This argument assumes that the "normal" profit level is the one calculated after spreading the cost over five years.

The reverse is true. The "normal" profit is the one calculated after accounting for all actual expenses incurred in the period. If a business spends USD 25,000 on a vehicle, its profit is reduced by USD 25,000. That is not distortion. That is fact.

The distortion occurs when the income statement shows only USD 5,000 of that expense, inflating the reported profit by USD 20,000. A stakeholder reviewing that statement is misled into believing the business is more profitable than it actually is — in that specific period. Over the five years, the total expense is the same, but in each individual year, the depreciation method misrepresents reality.

## 8. AAOIFI's Position — Adoption Without Scrutiny

### What Is AAOIFI?

The Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions (AAOIFI) is the primary standard-setting body for Islamic finance. Established in 1990 and headquartered in Bahrain, AAOIFI issues Financial Accounting Standards (FAS) that govern how Islamic financial institutions report their transactions.

### AAOIFI's Treatment of Depreciation

AAOIFI's Financial Accounting Standard 13 (FAS 13) — "Property, Plant and Equipment" — addresses asset recognition, measurement, and depreciation. The standard largely mirrors IAS 16 (IFRS) in its treatment:

| AAOIFI FAS 13 Position | Comparison to IFRS IAS 16 |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Assets recognised at cost | Identical to IAS 16 |
| Depreciation over useful life | Identical to IAS 16 |
| Choice of depreciation method | Identical to IAS 16 |
| Impairment testing required | Identical to IAS 16 |
| Land not depreciated | Identical to IAS 16 |
| Residual value estimated | Identical to IAS 16 |

**AAOIFI has adopted the conventional depreciation framework with no substantive modification for Islamic principles.**

### The Failure to Interrogate

AAOIFI was established to develop accounting standards that reflect Islamic values and principles. On the matter of depreciation, it has done no such thing. It has adopted the IFRS framework and applied an Islamic label without examining whether the underlying assumptions are compatible with:

- The Quranic command to record transactions **bil-adl** (in justice/truth)
- The prohibition of **gharar** in financial dealings
- The principle of **yadan bi yad** (hand-to-hand settlement)
- The broader Islamic emphasis on **transparency and honest dealing**

This is not a minor oversight. Depreciation affects every balance sheet of every Islamic financial institution worldwide. The reported asset values, profit figures, and financial positions of these institutions are all calculated using a method that has never been evaluated against Islamic first principles.

### Does AAOIFI Follow IFRS or Maintain Independence?

AAOIFI maintains formal independence from IFRS and GAAP. It issues its own standards. However, in practice, the majority of AAOIFI's financial accounting standards are adapted from IFRS with Shariah-specific modifications applied only where the transaction type is distinctly Islamic (murabaha, ijara, sukuk, etc.).

For general accounting treatments like depreciation, inventory valuation, and revenue recognition, AAOIFI defaults to conventional frameworks. The assumption appears to be that these are "neutral" accounting mechanics with no Shariah implications.

This paper challenges that assumption directly. Depreciation is not a neutral mechanic. It is a framework built on estimation, projection, and deferred recognition — all of which conflict with Islamic principles of truthfulness and certainty in financial record-keeping.

## 9. Counter-Arguments Addressed

### "Immediate Expensing Creates Volatile Income Statements"

**The argument:** If a business makes a large asset purchase, immediate expensing creates an artificially low profit in that year and artificially high profits in subsequent years. This volatility makes financial statements less useful for decision-making.

**The response:** The volatility is real. The cash outflow actually happened. Smoothing it across five years does not eliminate the volatility — it hides it. A decision-maker who sees the actual expense can plan accordingly. A decision-maker who sees the smoothed version is operating on incomplete information.

Furthermore, if income is genuinely high enough to support the asset purchase, the income statement absorbs the expense without showing distress. If the expense creates a loss, that loss is an accurate representation of the business's financial position in that period. Hiding it serves no one.

### "The Matching Principle Requires Cost Allocation"

**The argument:** Revenue generated by an asset over its useful life should be matched with the cost of that asset over the same period. This produces a more meaningful profit figure.

**The response:** The matching principle is a convention, not a law of nature. It assumes a predictable relationship between asset cost and future revenue. This relationship is speculative. A delivery vehicle purchased today might generate high revenue in Year 1 and low revenue in Year 4 (or vice versa). The straight-line assumption that 20% of the cost corresponds to 20% of the revenue is arbitrary.

More importantly, the matching principle is a creation of conventional accounting theory. It has no basis in Islamic jurisprudence. The Islamic standard is not "match costs to revenues" — it is "record what happened, honestly."

### "The Balance Sheet Must Reflect Asset Values"

**The argument:** Without depreciation, the balance sheet would not show the declining value of assets over time, giving a misleading picture of the business's worth.

**The response:** Depreciation does not reflect the actual value of assets. It reflects a mathematical formula applied to the original cost. A vehicle with a book value of USD 15,000 (after 2 years of straight-line depreciation on a USD 25,000 purchase) might have a market value of USD 10,000 or USD 19,000. The book value is not the market value. It is not the replacement value. It is not the liquidation value. It is a calculated number with no direct correspondence to any real-world value.

If the balance sheet must reflect asset values, the appropriate method is periodic revaluation to fair market value — not a formulaic write-down based on assumptions made at the time of purchase.

### "Tax Authorities Require Depreciation"

**The argument:** Tax codes in most jurisdictions require depreciation for calculating taxable income. Immediate expensing is not permitted.

**The response:** This is a valid practical constraint, and this paper does not argue against compliance with local tax law. However, tax compliance and truthful financial reporting are separate concerns. An Islamic institution can maintain its internal books on an immediate-expensing basis (reflecting the true economic reality) while making the adjustments required by tax authorities in a separate tax computation.

Many businesses already maintain dual records — management accounts and statutory accounts. The Islamic standard should govern the management accounts, which serve the institution's internal decision-making. Tax compliance is a separate obligation that does not override the duty of honest record-keeping.

It is also worth noting that several jurisdictions already permit or encourage immediate expensing. Section 179 in US tax law allows immediate deduction of qualifying asset purchases. The UK's Annual Investment Allowance permits 100% first-year deductions. The fact that some jurisdictions permit immediate expensing while others mandate depreciation demonstrates that the choice is policy, not principle.

### The Zakah Implication

This is a consequence that Islamic accounting professionals have not examined. Depreciation inflates reported profits by deferring real expenses. If **zakah** (زَكَاة) is calculated on reported profits — as it is in many Islamic institutions — then depreciation causes the institution to:

1. **Overstate its zakah base** — reported profits are higher than economic reality
2. **Potentially over-distribute** — dividends or distributions based on inflated profits drain cash
3. **Underprepare for asset replacement** — the books suggest the expense has been "managed" when the full replacement cost still lies ahead

The zakah question alone warrants formal examination by Shariah boards. If the accounting method itself produces numbers that distort the zakah calculation, then the method is not merely a technical convenience — it is a matter of religious obligation.

### "What If the Asset Is Destroyed Early?"

**The argument:** If an asset is expensed immediately and then destroyed in Year 1, the financial impact has already been fully absorbed. But under depreciation, the remaining book value must be written off as an impairment loss, creating a sudden charge.

**The response:** This argument actually supports immediate expensing. Under depreciation, the destruction of an asset creates a book loss that the financial statements were not prepared for — because the books showed an "asset" that no longer exists. Under immediate expensing, the destruction has no balance sheet impact. The expense was already recognised. The loss is purely physical, not financial.

The depreciation method creates a false sense of security that is shattered by unforeseen events. Immediate expensing accepts reality from the start.

## 10. The Connection to Riba — A Deeper Pattern

This research connects to a broader pattern identified in the companion document, [Interest-Free Currency Explained: Money Without the Debt Spiral](https://gxcoin.money/learn/interest-free-currency-explained).

### The Same Structural Flaw

Riba (interest) creates wealth from the passage of time on money that has already been lent. Depreciation creates expenses from the passage of time on money that has already been spent. Both mechanisms take a completed transaction and stretch it across time, creating obligations or effects that exist only on paper.

| Concept | Riba (Interest) | Depreciation |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| **What actually happened** | Money was lent | Money was spent on an asset |
| **What the books say** | The lender earns additional money over time | The expense is spread over time |
| **Time element** | Creates income from the passage of time | Creates expense from the passage of time |
| **Economic reality** | The loan was a single transaction | The purchase was a single transaction |
| **Gharar element** | Future income depends on borrower's ability to repay | Future expense depends on estimated useful life |
| **Settlement status** | Deferred — borrower owes more than received | Deferred — books show less expense than paid |

### The Time-Value Assumption

Both riba and depreciation are rooted in the same philosophical premise: **the time-value of money.** This is the idea that money today is worth more than money tomorrow, and that this differential justifies either charging for the use of money (interest) or spreading its cost over time (depreciation).

Islamic economics rejects this premise. Money does not have an inherent time value. Money has value only when it is exchanged for goods, services, or productive assets. The passage of time alone does not create value, and it does not create expense. A transaction occurs in the present. Its financial impact belongs in the present.

### The Debt-That-Is-Not-a-Debt

Depreciation creates what the author calls a "debt that is not a debt." After purchasing an asset for USD 25,000, the books show USD 20,000 in remaining "asset value." This functions psychologically like a debt — the business feels it must "recover" this value through future revenue. But there is no creditor. There is no obligation. The cash was paid. The transaction is complete.

This phantom obligation distorts decision-making. Management allocates resources as if they are servicing a multi-year cost, when in reality, the cost was fully borne on Day 1. The depreciation schedule creates an illusion of ongoing obligation where none exists — the same illusion that interest creates when it transforms a simple loan into a multi-year extraction mechanism.

## 11. Historical Precedent — The Bayt al-Māl

The recording systems used in the **Bayt al-Māl** (بَيْت المَال — the public treasury) during the Abbasid and Ottoman periods were predominantly cash-based. Income was recorded when received. Expenses were recorded when paid. The concept of spreading a paid expense over future periods would have been foreign to the scribes of the Bayt al-Māl, because it contradicts the observable reality of when the money left the treasury.

Cash-basis accounting for capital expenditures was, historically, the dominant method of bookkeeping before the rise of accrual accounting in the 19th and 20th centuries. Accrual accounting did not replace cash-basis because cash-basis was inaccurate — it was replaced because accrual accounting served the needs of large corporations, capital markets, and tax authorities who wanted smoother, more predictable financial reporting.

The Islamic commercial tradition — from the markets of Baghdad and Damascus to the trading houses of Zanzibar and Malacca — recorded transactions as they occurred. The scribe wrote what happened, when it happened. The idea that a completed cash payment should be algorithmically distributed across future periods is a product of 20th-century Western accounting theory, not of the Islamic recording tradition.

### The Accountant's Objection

When this policy was first communicated to the accounting executive, the response was: "This violates accounting and tax standards."

That objection is correct — it does depart from IFRS and local tax standards. What the objection fails to address is whether those standards are themselves correct from an Islamic perspective. The standards were designed for a conventional financial system built on interest, time-value-of-money assumptions, and accrual accounting. These foundations are not value-neutral. They carry embedded assumptions that conflict with Islamic economic principles.

An Islamic institution's first obligation is to its own principles. Compliance with secular accounting standards is secondary and can be achieved through supplementary disclosures or dual reporting. The primary books must tell the truth.

---

## 13. A Challenge to the Profession

This paper issues a direct challenge to three groups:

### To AAOIFI

Develop an independent standard for asset recognition and measurement that is derived from Islamic first principles — not adapted from IFRS. Examine whether the matching principle, the time-value-of-money assumption, and the concept of deferred cost allocation are compatible with the Quranic command to record transactions **bil-adl** and the Prophetic prohibition of gharar.

If AAOIFI's purpose is to create Islamic accounting standards, then the standards must be Islamic in their foundations — not merely IFRS with a Shariah overlay.

### To Islamic Finance Practitioners

Examine your own balance sheets. Every depreciated asset on your books represents a number that is neither the cost you paid, nor the market value you could recover, nor the cash you hold. It is a mathematical fiction derived from assumptions made years ago that may have no relationship to reality.

Ask: **Does this serve the Islamic duty of honest record-keeping?** If not, what are you going to do about it?

### To Shariah Scholars and Advisors

Issue a formal ruling on whether the assumptions embedded in conventional depreciation — estimated useful life, residual value, systematic cost allocation — constitute gharar when used in the financial statements of Islamic institutions. The profession has ruled on the permissibility of individual financial instruments. It has not ruled on the permissibility of the accounting framework used to record them.

The accounting framework is not neutral ground. It shapes every number on every financial statement of every Islamic institution worldwide. It deserves the same level of scrutiny as any financial product.

---

## 14. Conclusion

Depreciation is not a universal truth. It is a convention developed within a financial system that treats money as having an inherent time value, that accepts estimation as a basis for financial reporting, and that prioritises smoothed income statements over factual record-keeping.

Islamic principles demand a different standard: **record what happened, when it happened, as it happened.** An asset purchase is a single, complete transaction. Its full cost belongs in the period in which the cash was paid and the asset was received. Spreading that cost across future periods introduces uncertainty (gharar), misrepresents the financial position (violation of bil-adl), and treats a settled transaction as an ongoing obligation (violation of yadan bi yad).

For 19 years, this approach has produced financial statements that are transparent, aligned with cash reality, and free from the assumptions and projections that conventional depreciation requires.

The question is not whether this approach is correct. The evidence speaks for itself.

The question is: **why has the Islamic accounting profession never asked whether the conventional approach is wrong?**
