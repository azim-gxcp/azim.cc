# Scenario Matrix — Characters × Stories

This matrix maps common GX Protocol educational scenarios to the characters most suited to tell them. Use it as a starting point when drafting a video script.

**Convention**: **Bold** = protagonist(s) of the scene. Regular = supporting roles. Characters named here are canonical — once cast, they stay in-role across the entire series.

---

## Part 1 — Foundational Adoption Scenarios

### Scenario 1.1 — The First Grant
*A young person turns 18, registers, completes KYC, and receives their first monthly grant.*

| Role | Character |
|------|-----------|
| First-time recipient | **Lina** (student, 19) |
| Explainer | **Maya** (teacher) |
| KYC reviewer | **Iris** (KYC reviewer) |
| Younger sibling looking on | Kofi (high school student) |
| Narrator bridge | Nova (educator) |

**Story beat**: Lina receives GX 500. She holds purchasing power — but the local shops still price in fiat. The story ends with a question: "what can I spend it on?" — setting up Scenario 1.2.

---

### Scenario 1.2 — The First Merchant
*A neighborhood shop decides to accept GX. Foot traffic changes. A tipping point begins.*

| Role | Character |
|------|-----------|
| First merchant | **Zara** (grocer) |
| Partner onboarder | **Blaze** (partner onboarder) |
| Early customers | Lina, Maya, Sam |
| Bookkeeper helping her track it | Priya |
| Skeptical shopkeeper next door | (implied off-screen, returns in later scenario) |

**Story beat**: Zara takes a small risk. Her first GX customer is Lina. The next is Maya, who has been waiting to spend. Within a week, three shops on the street accept GX. The velocity begins.

---

### Scenario 1.3 — The Chicken-and-Egg Problem Resolved
*Explaining how holders-without-merchants and merchants-without-holders solve simultaneously as the grant pool grows.*

| Role | Character |
|------|-----------|
| The audience-proxy skeptic | **Yuki** (economics student) |
| The explainer | **Ellis** (economist) |
| The evidence | Arun selling to Zara, Zara paying Kai, Kai buying from Elsa |
| Narrator | Nova |

**Story beat**: Yuki challenges the model. Ellis walks through the math — monthly grants create a price floor of holders; merchant adoption reaches escape velocity when ~N participants per district have received at least one grant. The chain of transactions is shown as a visual flow.

## Part 2 — Velocity, Tax, and Holding

### Scenario 2.1 — Why Holders Spend
*Velocity tax explained through a participant who hoards vs. one who circulates.*

| Role | Character |
|------|-----------|
| The hoarder | **Cruz** (trader — tries to hold GX for gain, discovers the velocity tax) |
| The circulator | **Sam** (delivery rider — small frequent transactions) |
| Bookkeeper showing the numbers | Priya |
| Economist framing it | Ellis |

**Story beat**: Cruz holds GX expecting it to appreciate. The velocity tax erodes his holding. Sam, who circulates GX daily, pays effectively nothing. The lesson: GX rewards participation, not accumulation.

---

### Scenario 2.2 — Storing Wealth vs. Transacting in GX
*GX is a medium of exchange; wealth stores in productive assets.*

| Role | Character |
|------|-----------|
| The questioner | **Quinn** (investment analyst) |
| The answerer | **Ellis** (economist) |
| Living example | **Tara** (real estate) — buyer prices a property, paid partially in GX |
| The buyer | Jordan (café owner expanding) |

**Story beat**: Quinn asks whether GX is a store of value. Ellis reframes: GX is a *stable unit of exchange*. Stored wealth belongs in productive assets — land, businesses, tools. Tara shows a property sale where GX facilitates the transaction; the wealth lives in the building.

## Part 4 — Lending & Capital

### Scenario 4.1 — Business Loan Pool vs. Bank Loan
*A small business needs expansion capital. Two paths.*

| Role | Character |
|------|-----------|
| The business owner | **Jordan** (café) |
| The banker's path | **Max** (banker) — loan terms, collateral, interest |

### Scenario 4.2 — Venture Capital vs. Business Loan Pool
*A scalable startup needs growth capital. VC is one shape; the pool is another.*

| Role | Character |
|------|-----------|
| The founder | Theo (software engineer — building a product) |
| VC path | **Vera** (VC) — equity, board, exit pressure |
| Pool path | **Blaze** + Sage (treasury steward) |
| Economist framing the two | Ellis |

**Story beat**: Theo's product needs GX 1,000,000. Vera offers the deal. Sage walks through the pool's terms. The video concludes: the two paths are for different kinds of businesses. VC is the right fit for some; the pool is the right fit for others. GX is not anti-VC — it offers an alternative path for ventures that do not need or want equity dilution.

## How to Use This Matrix

1. **Pick a teaching point** (e.g., "velocity tax").
2. **Find the scenarios that carry it** (2.1, 7.3).
3. **Use the character cast provided as a default**. You can substitute within the cast — any teacher scene can use Maya; any banker scene can use Max.
4. **Keep character roles fixed**. Maya is always a teacher. Max is always a banker. Never reassign.
5. **Build visual continuity** — returning locations (Jordan's café, Arun's farm, Rune's validator room) anchor the series.
