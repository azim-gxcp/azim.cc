## 1. Campaign Strategy

### Objective

Share the personal intellectual journey of building a revenue model for a global payment protocol — from AAM's personal LinkedIn account (400+ followers/connections). The tone is personal, vulnerable, and inviting. This is one person's 27-month challenge, shared to educate, learn, and mutually benefit from the expertise of the network.

### Important: Personal Page, Not Company Page

This is NOT a corporate announcement. This is a person sharing what they have been working on for 27 months — the struggles, the discoveries, the numbers that seemed impossible, and the intellectual honesty of publishing weaknesses. The voice is "I", not "we" (except when referring to the team). The goal is mutual learning, not promotion.

### The 4-Post Series (Split Post 1)

LinkedIn polls allow only **140 characters for the question** and **30 characters per option**. This means the teaser story and the poll must be separate posts. This is actually an advantage — two touchpoints on Day 1, doubling feed visibility.

| Post | Format | Content | Image | Timing |
|------|--------|---------|-------|--------|
| **Post 1A** | **Text + Image** | Full teaser story — personal 27-month journey, the number that looked wrong, 88% score, "poll coming next" | Journey visual | Day 1 |
| **Post 1B** | **Poll + Image** | Short poll question, 4 concise options, brief reference back to 1A | Poll visual | Day 1 (1-2 hours later) or Day 2 |
| **Post 2** | **Text + Image** | Full narrative of winning poll option | Data-driven story visual | Day 4-6 (after poll closes) |
| **Post 3** | **Text + Image** | Publish assumptions, invite crowd modelling | Open-book / scrutiny visual | Day 8-11 |

### Key Principles

1. **Personal voice** — "I", not "we" (except when referring to the team)
2. Lead with the number, not the project name
3. Show the struggle, the doubt, the rebuilding — not the polished conclusion
4. The 27 months is the credential — it signals personal commitment and depth
5. The model is defensible but not finished — invite expertise as mutual learning
6. Every post gets a companion image
7. **Never promotional** — this is education and invitation, not marketing
8. **Post 1A and 1B work together** — teaser builds context, poll captures intent

## 2. Post 1A: The Teaser Story (Text + Image)

### Post Text (Ready to Publish — Personal Voice, ~2,800 characters)

---

27 months ago I started building a global payment protocol from scratch.

Not a whitepaper exercise. Not a weekend project. 27 months of designing the economic architecture, the identity framework, the lending model, the velocity mechanism, the governance structure — questioning every assumption, tearing down what I had built, and rebuilding it when the logic did not hold up.

Along the way, I scored the overall economic model at 88% — not 100% — because I wanted to be honest about what I had not yet solved. I published the weaknesses before anyone asked me to.

A few weeks ago, I turned to the revenue model — how validators earn, how partners profit, how the economics sustain the infrastructure. This is where the numbers got interesting.

The first revenue calculation returned a number so large I assumed I had made an error. I checked the arithmetic three times. I changed every assumption — fewer participants, smaller transactions, lower fees.

The number fell. But it did not become ordinary.

It took five iterations over two intensive weeks to understand what I was looking at. I was not modelling a niche blockchain. I was modelling a fraction of what the world already transacts every single day.

Visa processes 712 million transactions per day. India's UPI handles 675 million. Brazil's PIX does 276 million. SWIFT moves USD 5 trillion daily. My model assumed a fraction of these volumes — and even that fraction produced numbers I could not believe until I traced every input back to its published source.

The revenue model covers two very different stories. One is about validators — the infrastructure operators who secure the network. The other is about partners — the financial service providers who deploy capital and serve participants. Both went through the same process: a first calculation that looked impossibly large, followed by weeks of trying to disprove it, followed by the realisation that the numbers were not wrong — my frame of reference was.

Five iterations in, the model is defensible. But I am not declaring it finished. I have been working on the protocol for over two years, and I know that the best version of any model is the one that has been challenged by people who know things I do not.

If you have expertise in payment economics, monetary systems, financial infrastructure, or you simply enjoy tearing apart financial models — I would genuinely value your perspective. Not to validate what I have built, but to find what I have missed.

I am going to put up a poll shortly to ask what you would like to see first. In the meantime, if any of this resonates — or if you think I am completely off base — I would welcome that conversation too.

## 4. Post 2 Templates: The Winning Story

### Template A: If "Validator Story" Wins

**Post Text (Personal Voice):**

---

Last week I asked what you wanted to hear about first. The validator economics won. Here is the story — and the honest struggle behind it.

The first time I ran the validator revenue calculation, the number came back at USD 41.5 million per validator per year.

I assumed I had made an error. I checked the arithmetic. I checked it again. I changed every assumption — fewer participants, smaller transactions, lower fees. The number fell, but it did not become ordinary.

I spent three weeks pulling the model apart. What I eventually understood was that I had not made an error in arithmetic. I had made an error in perspective. I was not modelling a niche blockchain. I was modelling a fraction of what Visa (712 million transactions/day), UPI (675 million/day), and SWIFT (USD 5 trillion/day) already process.

The model changed five times because I refused to accept a number I could not defend:
- v1.0: 40% of tx fees → USD 41.5M per validator. I knew this was wrong.
- v1.2: Pulled to 10% → USD 10.4M. Still did not feel right.
- v1.4: Pulled to 5% → USD 7.28M per single cluster (Year 5). This felt grounded.
- v1.5: Added 99.99% SLA obligations → USD 7.28M per cluster. This I could defend.

A validator invests USD 75K (onboarding + 3 redundant servers). Breaks even within the first Year 5 distribution period. Accumulates USD 37.5M–50.0M in net profit over 10 years per cluster. Can operate up to 10 clusters — making this a viable primary business at USD 72.8M per year.

For comparison — what it costs elsewhere:
- Ethereum: lock USD 60-80K in capital for 3.5-4.2% yield
- Solana: USD 60-80K/year in operating costs
- This protocol: USD 0 locked, USD 13-15K/year operating cost, USD 7.28M/year per cluster at Year 5, 99.99% SLA

I did not invent these numbers. I discovered them — by applying published transaction volumes from Visa, Mastercard, SWIFT, UPI, and PayPal to a fee schedule and doing multiplication.

This model is not finished. If you have built payment infrastructure or run validator nodes, I would genuinely learn from your perspective. Where would you poke holes?

Next week: the partner story, or the full assumption set — you tell me.

## 6. Engagement Tactics

### During Poll Phase (Post 1)

- **Do not engage in arguments.** If someone is skeptical: "That is exactly the right instinct. I had the same reaction when I first saw the numbers. The full story addresses it — including what I think I got wrong."
- **Reply to every substantive comment** within 24 hours — speed signals you are genuinely present, not broadcasting
- **No promotional language** in replies — facts and personal reflections only
- **If someone asks for details early**: "I am putting the final touches on it. I want to make sure every number is traceable before I share it."
- **If someone offers expertise**: "I would genuinely appreciate that. Can I DM you?" — build 1:1 relationships, not broadcast engagement

### During Story Phase (Post 2)

- **Pin a comment** with source references (Visa annual report, GSMA SOTIR, Safaricom FY2025)
- **If challenged**: respond with the source and calculation — "Here is where that number comes from: [source]. If you see it differently, I want to understand why."
- **If "too good to be true"**: "I had the same reaction. I spent three weeks trying to disprove it. Here is the specific assumption you can check: [link]."
- **Reinforce the openness**: "This model is not finished. Five iterations deep. I am looking for the sixth — and it might come from this conversation."

### During Challenge Phase (Post 3)

- **Share assumptions in usable format** — downloadable table or spreadsheet, not a PDF wall
- **Respond to every model submission** with specific, technical feedback — show you actually read it
- **If someone finds a genuine flaw**: acknowledge publicly and update. Say: "You were right. I have updated the model. Thank you." This is the highest-credibility action possible on LinkedIn.
- **Collect contacts** from people who want ongoing participation — these are not followers, they are your first serious stakeholders and potential advisors
