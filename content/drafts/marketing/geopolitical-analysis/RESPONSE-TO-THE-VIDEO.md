## What Simon Dixon Described

Simon Dixon, with Peter McCormack, laid out a structural map of how the global financial system operates. Not who runs it in the conspiratorial sense — but how the mechanics work. The incentive structures. The extraction mechanisms. The feedback loops.

Some of what he described is verifiable from primary sources. Some is his analysis and interpretation. I am not endorsing every claim. But the structural framework — the underlying mechanics — aligns with what years of independent analysis have shown me.

Let me be specific about what resonated.

---

### "Socialize the losses, privatize the gains"

This is not rhetoric. It is documented.

In 2008, the US government directed USD 700 billion in taxpayer funds to bail out the financial institutions that caused the crisis. The Federal Reserve provided an additional USD 16.1 trillion in emergency lending (GAO audit, 2011). Zero senior bank executives were criminally prosecuted. The six largest US banks emerged 37% larger than before the crisis.

In 2020, central banks collectively created approximately USD 10 trillion in new money. The S&P 500 rose 114% from its March 2020 low. US billionaire wealth increased by USD 1.3 trillion in the first twelve months. Meanwhile, cumulative inflation eroded the purchasing power of everyone who held cash.

The pattern is simple: when the bets pay off, the profits are private. When the bets fail, the losses are distributed to everyone through bailouts and inflation. The top 10% of US households own 93% of equities (Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances, 2023). When asset prices are inflated by monetary policy, 93% of the benefit flows upward.

This is not opinion. These are published numbers from the institutions themselves.

---

### "Those that own assets and those that are the assets"

Dixon draws a line between people who protect their wealth by holding assets — real estate, equities, commodities — and people whose labour and savings are eroded by the same inflationary system that enriches asset holders.

The US dollar has lost approximately 87% of its purchasing power since 1971 (Bureau of Labor Statistics CPI calculator). A teacher who saved USD 10,000 in 1971 has roughly USD 1,300 in purchasing power today. She did nothing wrong. She saved. The system punished her for it.

Every central bank in the world has an explicit inflation target — typically 2% annually. This is a policy decision. It is a deliberate choice to erode the purchasing power of every person who saves in that currency. The stated rationale is to "encourage spending and investment." The structural effect is a wealth transfer from savers to borrowers — and the largest borrower is always the government.

The question this raises is fundamental: should anyone have the power to dilute someone else's savings? If the answer is no, then the design of money must change. Not the regulation of money. The design.

---

### The Three Complexes: Military, Financial, Technological

Eisenhower warned about the Military Industrial Complex in 1961. Dixon extends the framework to include the Financial Industrial Complex and the Technical Industrial Complex — and maps how the three coordinate.

What struck me is not the conspiracy dimension — it is the structural dimension. Each complex exists because of incentive alignment, not secret coordination:

- **Military spending** creates jobs in congressional districts. Politicians who cut defence spending cut jobs. The cycle is self-reinforcing. US defence spending: USD 886 billion in FY2024.
- **Financial institutions** create money through lending. Governments depend on banks to buy their debt. Regulators are staffed by former bankers. The system regulates itself. Global debt: USD 315 trillion. Global money supply: approximately USD 150 trillion. The gap — USD 165 trillion — represents interest that must be paid from money that has not been created.
- **Technology companies** monetise data. Surveillance infrastructure creates dependency. Programmable money (CBDCs) gives issuers capabilities that have never existed — spending restrictions, expiry dates, geographic limits, account freezing without judicial process.

The convergence of these three — military funding through financial extraction, enforced through technological surveillance — is not a prediction. It is happening. 134 countries are exploring CBDCs (Atlantic Council CBDC Tracker). India's Aadhaar links 1.4 billion biometric IDs to financial services. Canada froze bank accounts during the 2022 trucker protest under the Emergencies Act — without court orders.

The question is not whether digital money will exist. It will. The question is who designs it, for whom, and with what constraints.

### Counterparty risk and the theft of time

Dixon makes an observation that I have felt but never articulated so clearly: every investment with counterparty risk steals your time. Board meetings. Legal disputes. Rogue founders. Compliance obligations. Monitoring. Reporting.

He sold all equity positions. He stopped investing in stocks, bonds, and index funds. He holds only self-custody Bitcoin and physical gold.

His reasoning: "Bitcoin in self-custody doesn't give me grief. Gold bars don't give me grief. You give me a loss issue, a board meeting, things that steal my time."

This is a profound reframing. We measure investment returns in percentages. We rarely measure the time cost — the hours, the anxiety, the cognitive load — of maintaining investments that depend on other people's decisions. When you calculate the true return on a private equity stake — profits minus time spent managing it — many investments have negative returns.

The principle extends beyond personal finance. Every institution, every intermediary, every counterparty in the financial chain introduces time cost and dependency. The question becomes: what would a financial system look like if it minimised counterparty risk to zero?

### "Exit the system"

This is where Dixon goes further than most commentators. He does not just critique the system — he leaves it.

He does not invest in the S&P 500. He does not hold bonds. He does not use BlackRock to manage his assets. His argument: if you invest in the index, you fund the military industrial complex. Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Palantir, General Dynamics — they are all in the index. If you receive yield from the financial system, you are complicit in the extraction mechanisms that generate it.

He invests instead in community businesses — farms, shops, schools. Real production. Real services. Real human needs.

This raises an uncomfortable question for anyone who claims to operate on ethical principles while investing in conventional financial instruments. Investment firms that market themselves on cooperative or ethical finance principles — and then deploy their capital into the same S&P 500, the same government bonds, the same corporate instruments — are participating in the system they claim to oppose. The marketing says "ethical." The portfolio says S&P 500.

Is that system exit? Or is it complicity with a label?

## The First Principles Connection

This is where the video intersected with work that I have done independently — and the convergence is what my friend knew would affect me.

### Dixon's Journey

Dixon describes how studying the structural mechanics of interest (usury) led him through monetary reform, Bitcoin, and eventually to reading ancient texts from a first-principles perspective. He did not start from religious conviction. He started from a question: why is the financial system structurally unsustainable?

His conclusion: the golden age of scholarship (8th-14th centuries) had established three pillars of sound economics:

1. **Free trade** — voluntary exchange without coercion
2. **Hard money** — a medium that cannot be debased
3. **Ethics** — a concept of what constitutes good and bad trade

Adam Smith adopted the first two. He stripped out the third — replacing ethics with "the invisible hand," the market as a self-regulating god. Dixon observes: "Adam Smith was on the twenty-pound note at the Bank of England — because this was part of the justification for worshipping markets in a debt-based Ponzi scheme."

What was lost when ethics was removed from economics? The concept that some trades are harmful regardless of whether both parties consent. The concept that money itself can be adulterated. The concept that a system which structurally guarantees that debt exceeds the money supply is not "efficient" — it is predatory.

### My Journey

I arrived at the same structural conclusions through a different path.

My published research — *"From Gold to Paper: The Characteristics of Ribawi Exchange and the Challenge of Fiat Currency"* — is a 120-page first-principles investigation. It begins from the Arabic language itself, from the three-letter roots of the relevant terms, and builds outward through textual evidence, historical context, and analytical reasoning.

The research identifies seven characteristics shared by the six items that classical scholarship recognised as proper mediums of exchange: gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt. It then applies those seven characteristics to modern fiat currency.

Fiat fails on virtually every count.

- **Fungibility**: A dollar today and a dollar in ten years carry the same denomination but represent radically different purchasing power. The equivalence is nominal, not real.
- **Measurability**: A dollar measures all other things, but who measures the dollar? The central bank — through its control of money supply and inflation targets. The instrument that measures all else cannot itself be measured. The measurement is circular.
- **Storability**: A medium of exchange that loses purchasing power by deliberate policy is not a store of value. It is a mechanism of erosion. The 2% inflation target is not a failure — it is the stated policy objective.
- **Intrinsic value**: Since 1971, fiat currency has no backing in any commodity. Its value derives entirely from government decree and collective trust.

The research concludes with five open questions, the most significant being: does the fiat monetary system itself constitute a structural form of adulteration — a debasement of the medium — that precedes and subsumes the interest charged within it?

In other words: is the entire system built on a diluted coin?

When Dixon describes his journey from monetary reform to first-principles understanding of why interest is structurally unsustainable — he is describing, from a different angle, the same intellectual journey my research documents. He arrived through Bitcoin and spiritual study. I arrived through Arabic linguistics and analytical rigour. We reached the same place.

## The Questions I Am Left With

### How much of this is true?

This is the question every honest person asks when confronted with claims about power structures. And it is the right question.

The answer is layered. Some of what Dixon describes is verified from primary sources — the bailout numbers, the Fed balance sheet expansion, the revolving door between Goldman Sachs and the Treasury, the Emergencies Act account freezes, the Eisenhower speech. These are public records.

Some is his interpretation — the three-faction model of corporate power, the claim that wars are negotiated outcomes, the specific dynamics of the Iran situation. These are analytical frameworks, not established facts.

The honest approach is to distinguish between the two: use the verified data, note the interpretive frameworks, and never present analysis as established truth.

### Who would believe an unknown individual?

Dixon discusses Chamath Palihapitiya's claim that 150 people run the world, and his ambition to aggregate enough capital to sit at that table.

I have no such ambition. I have no capital to aggregate. I have no political connections — not at the local level, not at the international level.

What I have is a body of work. A 120-page research paper built from first principles. A protocol specification. A soundness analysis. Years of design work.

History suggests that protocols are validated by their design, not their designer's credentials. The Web was proposed by an unknown physicist at CERN. Linux was released by a university student. Bitcoin was created by someone whose identity remains unknown to this day.

The work either holds up to scrutiny or it does not. That is all that matters.

### How do you communicate in a world like this?

Dixon describes a world where anyone who threatens the revenue streams of the existing power structure becomes a target. Gaddafi proposed a gold-backed African currency. Saddam began pricing oil in euros.

The lesson is clear: do not position yourself as an enemy. Position yourself as a question. "What if money were designed differently?" is not a threat. It is an invitation. An invitation to examine. An invitation to scrutinise. An invitation to participate — or not.

The strongest form of communication is not rhetoric. It is design. A system that works as described needs no marketing. It needs participants.
