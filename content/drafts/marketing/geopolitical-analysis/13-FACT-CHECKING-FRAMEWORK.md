# Fact-Checking Framework

> **Topic**: How to verify claims about financial power structures — what is established, what is contested, what is speculative
> **Purpose**: Every piece of content derived from this analysis must be sourced. This document establishes the verification hierarchy.
> **Key Question from AAM**: "How much of this discussion is actually true? How to fact check?"

---

## The Verification Problem

### "How would I know whether your answer is truth?"

This is the right question. When evaluating claims about financial power structures, there are three challenges:

1. **AI knowledge limitations**: AI models are trained on public data. We can identify and cite sources, but cannot independently verify classified or private information.

2. **Source reliability**: Even "authoritative" sources have biases. The BIS publishes data that serves its institutional interests. The Fed publishes data that supports its policy narrative. Independent researchers have their own perspectives.

3. **The conspiracy trap**: Claims about financial power exist on a spectrum from verifiable fact to unfounded speculation. The challenge is knowing where each claim sits.

## Source Hierarchy

### Tier 1: Primary Sources (Highest Confidence)

These are documents produced by the entities themselves, or legal/court records:

| Source Type | Examples | Why Reliable |
|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Legislation** | Federal Reserve Act, GENIUS ACT, Emergencies Act | Legally binding, publicly available |
| **Court records** | JPMorgan Epstein settlement, 2008 crisis investigations | Sworn testimony, legal findings |
| **Central bank data** | Fed balance sheet, BIS statistics, ECB decisions | Self-reported by the institution |
| **SEC filings** | Company annual reports, 10-K filings | Legally required, audited |
| **Government statistics** | BLS CPI, Census, Treasury debt data | Official statistical methodology |
| **Published speeches** | Eisenhower's farewell, Kashkari's "infinite cash" | Verbatim public record |

**Confidence**: These are facts. They may be incomplete or strategically framed, but the data itself is verifiable.

### Tier 2: Institutional Research (High Confidence)

| Source Type | Examples | Caveat |
|-------------|----------|--------|
| **Academic research** | Stigler's regulatory capture theory, peer-reviewed papers | May have methodological limitations |
| **International organisations** | World Bank (remittance data), IMF, SIPRI (military spending) | Institutional perspective, but data is methodologically rigorous |
| **Independent research organisations** | Tax Justice Network, OpenSecrets, Transparency International | Advocacy-adjacent but data-driven |
| **Investigative journalism** | ICIJ (Panama Papers, Pandora Papers), ProPublica | Verified documents, editorial oversight |

**Confidence**: High, but with acknowledged perspective. Use these sources and cite them specifically.

### Tier 3: Expert Analysis (Moderate Confidence)

| Source Type | Examples | Caveat |
|-------------|----------|--------|
| **Domain experts** | Economists, former regulators, industry analysts | Personal interpretation of data |
| **Think tanks** | Brookings, CATO, Atlantic Council | Known ideological leanings |
| **Financial media** | Bloomberg, Reuters, FT | Generally reliable, but can reflect sources' interests |
| **Books by researchers** | "Lords of Finance," "The Creature from Jekyll Island" | Varies widely in rigor |

**Confidence**: Moderate. Use as context, not as standalone evidence. Cross-reference with Tier 1 sources.

### Tier 4: Commentary and Analysis (Lower Confidence)

| Source Type | Examples | Caveat |
|-------------|----------|--------|
| **Podcast discussions** | Peter McCormack, Simon Dixon, Chamath | Personal opinions, not research |
| **Social media analysis** | Twitter threads, YouTube commentary | No editorial oversight |
| **Newsletters** | Substack, independent commentators | Quality varies enormously |
| **Documentary films** | "Inside Job," "The Big Short" | Entertaining but selective in framing |

**Confidence**: Use for identifying questions and themes, not as evidence. Always verify underlying claims against Tier 1-2 sources.

### Tier 5: Unverifiable Claims (Lowest Confidence)

| Source Type | Examples | Status |
|-------------|----------|--------|
| **Conspiracy theories** | "Fort Knox is empty," "the Illuminati control banking" | Unverifiable without evidence |
| **Anonymous sources** | "Insider says..." without documentation | Cannot be confirmed |
| **Extrapolations** | "Therefore, they must be..." | Speculation beyond evidence |
| **Historical revisionism** | Claims that rewrite established history without evidence | Treat with extreme caution |

**Confidence**: Do not use in any GX content. If a claim is interesting but unverifiable, note it as such and do not present it as fact.

## How to Verify Specific Claims from the Podcast

### Claims That Are Verified (Use Freely)

| Claim | Verification |
|-------|-------------|
| "Socialise losses, privatise gains" — the 2008 bailout pattern | TARP data (US Treasury), Fed emergency lending (GAO audit) |
| Federal Reserve is privately owned by member banks | Federal Reserve Act, Section 4 |

| Eisenhower warned about the MIC | Eisenhower Presidential Library, speech transcript |
| PayPal Mafia members lead major tech companies | Public career histories, SEC filings |
| BIS has sovereign immunity | BIS Host State Agreement, 1987 |
| City of London has a Remembrancer in Parliament | Parliamentary records |
| Canada froze accounts under Emergencies Act | Government Gazette, PC 2022-0106 |
| 134 countries exploring CBDCs | Atlantic Council CBDC Tracker |

### Claims That Are Partially Verified (Use Carefully)

| Claim | Status | How to Handle |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| "150 people run the world" (Chamath) | Exaggeration of a documented trend (wealth concentration) | Say: "Wealth and influence are heavily concentrated — three asset managers control USD 23T" |
| Gold reserves may have been leased | Possible but unconfirmed | Say: "The US has not conducted a full independent audit of gold reserves since 1953" |
| Epstein was connected to intelligence services | Circumstantial evidence, not proven | Say: "Court documents reveal extensive financial and social connections. Settlement amounts are public record." |
| CBDCs will be used for social control | Technologically possible, not yet implemented at scale | Say: "CBDC architecture enables programmable spending restrictions. Whether governments will use this capability is a policy question." |

### Claims That Are Speculative (Do NOT Use as Fact)

| Claim | Status | GX Response |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Fort Knox is empty | No evidence — absence of audit ≠ absence of gold | Do not reference |
| A shadowy group controls global finance | Unfalsifiable conspiracy framing | Use structural analysis instead |
| The pandemic was planned for financial benefit | No credible evidence | Focus on the monetary response, not the pandemic's origin |
| Specific individuals are part of a coordinated conspiracy | Unverifiable and potentially defamatory | Focus on structural incentives, not individual intent |

## The GX Fact-Checking Standard

### Before Publishing Any Content

1. **Identify every factual claim** in the content
2. **Assign each claim a tier** from the hierarchy above
3. **Only publish claims supported at Tier 1-3** as statements of fact
4. **Tier 4 claims** can be referenced as "according to [source]" or "as discussed by [commentator]"
5. **Tier 5 claims** must never appear in GX content
6. **Include source attribution** for all quantitative claims
7. **Use present tense for current data, past tense for historical events**

### The Golden Rule

**"If you cannot point to a specific, publicly available source for a claim, do not make it. If you can, cite it."**

GX's credibility depends on being more honest, more precise, and more transparent than the institutions it offers an alternative to. One unverified claim undermines everything.

---

## How to Respond When Asked "Is This True?"

Template response for unverifiable claims:

**"I cannot independently verify that claim. Here is what I can verify: [cite Tier 1-2 sources]. The unverified claim may be an interpretation of these facts, but the interpretation goes beyond the evidence. GX's position is based on what is documented, not what is speculated."**

This response:
- Acknowledges the question honestly
- Provides what IS verifiable
- Distinguishes fact from interpretation
- Positions GX as evidence-based
