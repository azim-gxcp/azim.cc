# Who Really Controls the Money — A Personal Response

**M Azim Abdul Majeed | April 2026**

---

A friend sent me a video last week — Simon Dixon in conversation with Peter McCormack on *What Bitcoin Did*. Two hours on the structural mechanics of the global financial system.

He knew what it would mean to me. He was right.

I have spent years studying these mechanics independently. Not the conspiracy version — the documented version. Federal Reserve balance sheets. BLS inflation data. GAO audit reports. Career histories published in official biographies. And a 120-page first-principles research paper I wrote on the structural characteristics of money, working from the Arabic language itself to examine whether modern fiat currency meets the classical criteria for a legitimate medium of exchange.

My conclusion: it does not. Fiat fails on fungibility (a dollar today and a dollar in ten years are not equivalent in any real sense), on measurability (the dollar measures everything, but who measures the dollar?), and on storability (a medium that loses purchasing power by deliberate policy is not a store of value — it is a mechanism of erosion).

Dixon arrived at the same structural conclusions through a completely different path — monetary reform, Bitcoin, and eventually studying ancient texts from a first-principles perspective. He describes how the golden age of scholarship established three pillars of sound economics: free trade, hard money, and ethics. Adam Smith kept the first two and stripped out the third, replacing ethics with "the invisible hand" — the market as god.

The convergence between his analysis and mine — reached independently, through different disciplines, from different starting points — is what made this video hit differently.

---

What Dixon describes is not theory. It is arithmetic.

Global debt: USD 315 trillion. Global money supply: approximately USD 100 trillion. The gap represents interest owed on money that was never created. The US dollar has lost 87% of its purchasing power since 1971. The top 10% of US households own 93% of equities. When central banks inflate asset prices through monetary expansion, 93% of the benefit flows upward. In 2020, central banks created approximately USD 10 trillion. Billionaire wealth increased by USD 1.3 trillion in twelve months. Everyone else got inflation.

These are not opinions. These are published numbers from the institutions themselves.

Dixon goes further than most. He does not just critique the system — he exits it. No S&P 500. No bonds. No BlackRock. His reasoning: if you invest in the index, you fund every defence contractor, surveillance company, and extraction mechanism in it. If you receive yield from the system, you are part of it.

He invests instead in community businesses — farms, shops, schools. Real production. Real human needs.

This raises a question I find uncomfortable and necessary: investment firms that market themselves on ethical or cooperative principles — and then deploy capital into the same S&P 500, the same government bonds — are they exiting the system, or participating in it with a label?

---

Dixon also maps what he calls the three complexes — Military, Financial, and Technological — and shows how each creates the conditions for the next. Military spending requires financing (FIC). Financial surveillance requires digital infrastructure (TIC). The convergence point is programmable money, digital identity, and AI-driven compliance. 134 countries are exploring CBDCs. India has linked 1.4 billion biometric IDs to financial services. Canada froze bank accounts during a political protest without court orders.

The question is not whether digital money will exist. It will. The question is who designs it, for whom, and with what constraints.

---

I watched this video and felt what I can only describe as recognition. Not agreement with every claim — Dixon's geopolitical analysis includes interpretation that goes beyond what primary sources confirm. But the structural diagnosis — the extraction mechanics, the incentive alignment, the mathematical impossibility of a system where total debt permanently exceeds the money supply — this is what I have been working on for years.

The difference is that I did not stop at diagnosis. I asked the design question: what would money look like if it preserved purchasing power instead of eroding it? If lending aligned incentives through shared outcomes instead of extracting through interest? If identity protected the individual instead of enabling surveillance? If access required only verified personhood, not a bank account, a credit score, or a minimum balance?

These are not rhetorical questions. They are engineering problems. And engineering problems have solutions.

I owe Simon Dixon and Peter McCormack an acknowledgement. Their conversation confirmed what independent research had shown me — that the structural defects in the monetary system are not opinions. They are mathematics.

And mathematics does not care about credentials, connections, or institutional backing. It either holds or it does not.
