## What Is Regulatory Capture?

Regulatory capture is the process by which a regulatory agency, created to act in the public interest, instead advances the commercial or political interests of the industry it is supposed to regulate.

This is not a conspiracy theory — it is a well-documented phenomenon studied in economics (George Stigler, Nobel laureate, 1971 paper "The Theory of Economic Regulation") and political science.

### How It Works

| Stage | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| **1. Expertise asymmetry** | The industry has more technical expertise than the regulator. Regulators must hire from the industry to be competent. |
| **2. Revolving door** | Regulators move to industry jobs after government service. The promise of lucrative future employment creates implicit incentive alignment. |
| **3. Lobbying** | Industry funds lobbying to shape regulation. In 2023, the US financial sector spent USD 721 million on lobbying (OpenSecrets). |
| **4. Information control** | Industry provides the data and analysis that regulators use to make decisions. |
| **5. Legislation drafting** | Industry lobbyists frequently draft the actual text of legislation that Congress votes on. |
| **6. Campaign funding** | Industry funds the election campaigns of politicians who sit on regulatory oversight committees. |

## The "Five Captured Governments" (Podcast Reference)

Simon Dixon explicitly names the five captured governments that make up the military industrial complex: **Germany, UK, France, Israel, and America**. He states: "Those governments print money, make sure it's spent into the stock market to prop up the systems — Palantir, Boeing, Lockheed Martin, General Dynamics, Howmet Aerospace, Raytheon — all of these different companies, and they need a brochure."

The five below are analysed using Dixon's framework combined with broader research:

### 1. United States

**Evidence of capture**:
- The Federal Reserve is technically a private institution owned by its member banks
- Treasury Secretaries regularly come from Goldman Sachs (Paulson, Mnuchin) or major financial institutions
- The 2008 bailout directed USD 700 billion to the institutions that caused the crisis
- Glass-Steagall repeal (1999) was lobbied for by Citigroup's Sandy Weill — who later said repealing it was a mistake
- Financial sector lobbying: USD 721 million in 2023 alone

**The structural lock**: Financial institutions fund campaigns → politicians appoint regulators → regulators come from the industry → regulations favour the industry → industry profits fund more lobbying.

### 2. United Kingdom

**Evidence of capture**:
- The City of London Corporation's Remembrancer sits in Parliament
- London is the world's leading centre for financial services and offshore finance
- UK Crown Dependencies (Jersey, Guernsey, Isle of Man) and Overseas Territories (Cayman Islands, BVI) form the world's largest offshore financial network
- The Tax Justice Network estimates USD 21-32 trillion is held in offshore tax havens, with UK-linked jurisdictions facilitating a significant share
- Financial services contribute approximately 12% of UK tax receipts — creating dependency

### 3. Germany

**Evidence of capture** (per Dixon's framework):
- Germany destroyed its own energy independence by shutting down nuclear plants and becoming dependent on imported energy (first Russian gas via Nord Stream, then US LNG after pipeline destruction)
- German military spending ramped up dramatically post-2022, funding NATO obligations that serve US military industrial interests
- Deutsche Bank — Germany's largest bank — settled for USD 225 million in Epstein-related cases and has been implicated in multiple money laundering scandals (Russian mirror trades, Danske Bank)
- Germany's manufacturing base is being hollowed out by energy costs — Dixon frames this as deliberate asset-stripping of European industry
- BlackRock's ESG framework pushed Chinese-manufactured wind and solar into European markets, displacing domestic industry

### 4. France

**Evidence of capture**:
- France's military operations in Africa (Françafrique) served corporate resource extraction interests for decades
- France maintained the CFA franc — a colonial-era currency pegged to the euro — across 14 African nations until recent pushback, extracting monetary sovereignty from former colonies
- French banks (BNP Paribas, Société Générale) have been fined billions for sanctions violations and money laundering
- NATO alignment has subordinated French strategic independence to collective Western military spending
- French nuclear energy expertise was sidelined in favour of imported renewables — Dixon frames this as part of the European energy dependency strategy

### 5. Israel

**Evidence of capture** (per Dixon's framework):
- Dixon describes Israel as "a subdivision of the military industrial complex" — a node in the transnational military-industrial network
- Functions as a testing ground for military technologies (surveillance, weapons systems, border control) that are then exported globally
- The relationship between US military aid to Israel (~USD 3.8 billion annually) and Israeli military technology purchases from US contractors creates a circular funding mechanism
- Dixon identifies Israel as one of the jurisdictions in the financial network alongside City of London, BIS, and Vatican
- "Israel first policy" provides "plausible deniability" narrative for broader military operations — "it's not us, it's them"
- Israeli tech sector (Unit 8200 alumni companies) deeply integrated with global surveillance infrastructure

**Note on framing**: These are Dixon's analytical claims. GX content should never target specific nations. Use structural analysis of how military-financial networks operate across borders.

## Beyond Nation-States: Institutional Capture

### International Institutions

| Institution | Capture Concern |
|-------------|----------------|
| **IMF** | Structural adjustment programmes historically benefited creditor nations and their banks at the expense of debtor nations |
| **World Bank** | Infrastructure lending that creates debt dependency; contracts often awarded to firms from donor nations |
| **WTO** | Trade rules that advantage developed economies with strong IP portfolios |
| **BIS** | Basel banking standards that entrench large banks and disadvantage smaller institutions |

### Rating Agencies as Captured Regulators

Moody's, S&P, and Fitch are paid by the entities they rate (issuer-pays model). Before 2008, they rated mortgage-backed securities AAA — the highest safety rating — days before those securities became worthless.

**The structural incentive**: If a rating agency gives a bank a low rating, the bank stops paying for ratings. The agency that rates most generously gets the most business.

### The Deeper Point

Regulatory capture is possible because regulation is discretionary — humans make judgement calls, and those judgement calls can be influenced. GX replaces discretionary regulation with protocol-defined constraints:

- **A central bank can decide to print money** → GX's supply is fixed in code
- **A regulator can grant exceptions** → GX's protocol has no exception mechanism
- **A politician can change the rules** → GX's rules change only through protocol governance (not political governance)
