> **Purpose**: Ready-to-use structures for articles, social media posts, and visual content derived from this analysis
> **Usage**: Select a template, fill in the specific topic, comply with `00-CONTENT-GUIDELINES.md`

## Article Template 1: "The Structure Behind the Number"

**Best for**: LinkedIn articles, Medium posts
**Length**: 800-1,200 words
**Structure**:

```
TITLE: A single surprising number

OPENING (100 words):
State a specific, verifiable number that reveals a structural truth.
Do not explain it yet. Let it sit.

CONTEXT (200 words):
Explain where the number comes from — the system, the mechanism, the history.
Use Tier 1-2 sources. Be precise.

THE STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS (300 words):
Explain WHY this number exists. Not who is to blame — but what structural
incentive produces this outcome. Use the language of systems, not accusations.

THE DESIGN QUESTION (200 words):
"What if this were designed differently?" Introduce GX's specific design
element that addresses this structural issue. Be concrete — name the
mechanism, the parameter, the rule.

THE HUMAN IMPACT (100 words):
One composite participant story. What changes for this person.

CLOSING (100 words):
Restate the number. Contrast it with GX's alternative. End with a question
that invites the reader to examine further.
```

**Example Topics**:
- "USD 315 trillion" — global debt vs money supply
- "87%" — USD purchasing power lost since 1971
- "USD 48 billion" — annual remittance fee extraction
- "USD 10 trillion" — central bank money creation during pandemic
- "93%" — share of stocks owned by top 10%

## Article Template 2: "Two Systems, One Choice"

**Best for**: Comparative analysis, decision-oriented content
**Length**: 1,000-1,500 words
**Structure**:

```
TITLE: Name the two systems being compared

OPENING (150 words):
Present a scenario — a real person making a real financial decision.
Show them facing a choice between two systems.

SYSTEM A — THE CURRENT WAY (300 words):
Describe how the current system handles this scenario.
Include specific costs, time, intermediaries, data exposure.
Use real numbers.

SYSTEM B — THE GX WAY (300 words):
Describe how GX handles the same scenario.
Same level of specificity — costs, time, process.
Use real numbers from the protocol specification.

THE COMPARISON TABLE (visual):
Side-by-side feature comparison. Let the data speak.

THE STRUCTURAL REASON (200 words):
Why does System A cost more / take longer / expose more data?
Not because of bad people — because of structural design decisions
made in a different era for different purposes.

THE CHOICE (150 words):
Do not tell the reader what to choose. Present both options
with full transparency. End with: "The choice is yours."
```

**Example Comparisons**:
- Remittance: traditional banking vs GX
- Savings: Turkish Lira vs GX (inflation analysis)
- Lending: 18% bank loan vs GX interest-free with profit-sharing
- Privacy: CBDC vs GX
- Inheritance: Bitcoin (lost keys) vs GX (identity-based recovery)

## Article Template 3: "What [Credible Source] Revealed — And What It Means"

**Best for**: Leveraging external events, reports, or statements
**Length**: 600-900 words
**Structure**:

```
TITLE: Reference the source and the revelation

OPENING (100 words):
Quote or paraphrase the source directly. Cite it.
(Eisenhower's speech, Kashkari's "infinite cash," a BIS report, a World Bank study)

WHAT THEY SAID (150 words):
Present the source material faithfully. Context matters.

WHAT IT MEANS STRUCTURALLY (250 words):
Analyse the structural implication. Not "this proves corruption" —
but "this reveals a structural incentive that produces X outcome."

THE GX PERSPECTIVE (200 words):
How does GX's design address this structural issue?
Be specific about the mechanism.

THE QUESTION (100 words):
End with a question that reframes the reader's understanding.
```

**Example Sources**:
- Kashkari: "There is an infinite amount of cash in the Federal Reserve" (2020)
- Eisenhower: Military-industrial complex warning (1961)
- GAO: USD 16.1 trillion in Fed emergency lending (2011 audit)
- World Bank: Remittance fee data (annual)
- Tax Justice Network: Financial Secrecy Index (biennial)
- Atlantic Council: CBDC Tracker (ongoing)

## Article Template 4: "The Question Nobody Is Asking"

**Best for**: Thought leadership, paradigm-shifting content
**Length**: 500-800 words
**Structure**:

```
TITLE: The question itself

OPENING (100 words):
Present a widely accepted assumption.
"Everyone agrees that [X]."

THE QUESTION (50 words):
"But what if [X] is not a law of nature? What if it is a design choice?"

THE EVIDENCE (250 words):
Show that the assumption IS a design choice — with historical context.
When was this decided? By whom? What was the alternative?

THE ALTERNATIVE DESIGN (200 words):
Present GX's answer. Not as "better" — but as "different."
"GX chose differently. Here is what that looks like."

THE INVITATION (100 words):
"We are not asking you to believe us. We are asking you to
examine the assumption. Here is the specification. Here is the
soundness analysis. If you find flaws, we want to know."
```

**Example Questions**:
- "What if 2% inflation is not a natural law?"
- "What if lending without interest is not utopian?"
- "What if money didn't need a bank to reach you?"
- "What if the people who create money had no right to profit from it?"
- "What if privacy and compliance were not opposites?"

## Social Media Post Templates

### LinkedIn Post (1,500-2,250 characters)

```
[Surprising fact or number — 1 sentence]

[Why this matters — 2-3 sentences with context]

[The structural reason this exists — 2-3 sentences]

[The GX perspective — 2-3 sentences, framed as a design question]

[Closing question for engagement]

#GXProtocol #FinancialInfrastructure #PublicUtility
```

### X/Twitter Post (280 characters)

```
[Number]. [What it means for one person]. [The question nobody asks.]
```

### Instagram Carousel (5-7 slides)

```
Slide 1: Bold number or statement (hook)
Slide 2: Context — where does this number come from?
Slide 3: The structural mechanism (simple diagram)
Slide 4: Who benefits, who pays
Slide 5: The alternative design (GX)
Slide 6: The human impact
Slide 7: Call to examine (not call to action)
```
