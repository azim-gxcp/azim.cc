## POST A1 - THE DEBT GAP

USD 315 trillion in global debt. Approximately USD 150 trillion in global money supply.

Pause on that. The world owes more than twice the amount of money that exists.

Where does the gap come from? Interest. When a bank issues a loan, it creates the principal. It does not create the interest owed on that principal. That interest must be paid from money that someone else borrows into existence, which itself carries interest.

The result is a system in which total debt structurally and permanently exceeds the total money supply. Not because of bad decisions or irresponsible borrowers. Because of the mathematics of how money is created.

This is not a market failure. This is the design. Money enters circulation through debt. Debt carries interest. Interest compounds. The gap widens.

In such a system, default is not an anomaly. It is a mathematical certainty for some portion of participants. There is simply not enough money in existence for everyone to repay what is owed.

The question this raises is not political. It is structural: can a monetary system built on debt ever produce a stable equilibrium? Or does the mathematics guarantee that wealth concentrates and debt accumulates until the system resets?

Every major financial crisis in modern history has been, at its core, a partial reset of this gap. And after every reset, the cycle begins again.

Sources: IIF Global Debt Monitor 2024; central bank aggregate M2 data.

## POST A2 - THE 87% EROSION

One thousand dollars, saved in 1971. Purchasing power today: approximately one hundred and thirty dollars.

That is not a market outcome. That is a policy outcome.

In 1971, the United States ended the convertibility of the dollar to gold. Since that moment, the dollar has been backed by nothing but government decree and collective trust. In the same period, every major central bank adopted an explicit inflation target, typically two percent per year.

Two percent sounds modest. Compounded over fifty years, it erases eighty-seven percent of purchasing power.

This is not a failure of the system. It is the stated objective. Central banks publish their inflation targets. They hold press conferences about them. They adjust interest rates to achieve them. The erosion is deliberate, documented, and defended as necessary to "encourage spending and investment."

Consider what that means for a teacher, a nurse, a construction worker who saves a portion of their wages each month. They are not making a mistake. They are doing what every financial advisor tells them to do. And the system punishes them for it, by design, at two percent per year.

The question is simple: should anyone have the authority to erode someone else's savings as a matter of policy? And if the answer is no, what does the design of money need to change?

## POST A3 - THE FEE CHAIN

A domestic worker in Dubai sends two hundred dollars home to her family in Manila. The fee is fourteen dollars. Seven percent of her transfer, extracted by intermediaries.

Globally, migrant workers lose between forty-eight and fifty-three billion dollars every year to remittance fees. Not to taxes. Not to bad decisions. To friction. The structural inefficiency of a system that profits from sitting between sender and receiver.

The fee chain on a single remittance typically passes through five intermediaries: the sender's bank, a correspondent bank, a currency exchange, the receiving bank, and a local agent. Each takes a cut. The total cost: three to seven percent, depending on the corridor.

SWIFT processes over five trillion dollars in daily transactions. Visa and Mastercard handle over eighty percent of global card payments. The infrastructure to move money instantly exists. The fees exist because the incentive structure rewards intermediaries, not because the technology demands them.

Fourteen dollars is a day's wages for that domestic worker. Multiplied across the world's 281 million international migrants, the extraction is staggering.

The design question: what if the sender and receiver were on the same system? No conversion. No correspondent bank chain. No spread captured by an exchange bureau. Just a transfer, for a fraction of a percent.

The technology to build this exists today. The reason it has not been built is not technical. It is structural. The intermediaries profit from the friction.

Sources: World Bank Remittance Prices Worldwide 2024; SWIFT annual report; UN Migration Report 2024.

The structure is consistent: when bets pay off, profits are private. When bets fail, losses are distributed to the public through bailouts and currency debasement.

The question: in what other domain of human activity do we accept a system where the risk-taker keeps the reward and someone else absorbs the loss?

## POST A5 - INFINITE CASH

"There is an infinite amount of cash in the Federal Reserve."

Neel Kashkari, President of the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis. CBS 60 Minutes. March 2020.

He said it plainly. On national television. Without qualification.

And he was telling the truth. The Federal Reserve can create an unlimited quantity of dollars. There is no constitutional constraint, no mathematical limit, no physical boundary. The limit is political will and the tolerance of the public for the inflation that follows.

Between March 2020 and December 2021, the Federal Reserve's balance sheet expanded from four point two trillion dollars to eight point nine trillion. That is four point seven trillion dollars created from nothing, used primarily to purchase government bonds and mortgage-backed securities.

Where did that money go? Into financial markets first. Asset prices surged. The S&P 500 doubled. Housing prices rose forty-five percent. Those who held assets before the creation became wealthier. Those who held cash saw their purchasing power decline.

This is not an allegation. This is the Federal Reserve's own published balance sheet data, available on the FRED database to anyone who cares to look.

The question that haunts me: if the supply of money is infinite, what does it mean to save? What does it mean to earn? What does it mean to plan for the future in a medium that can be diluted without limit by an authority you did not elect?

## POST A6 - THE 93/7 SPLIT

The top ten percent of US households own ninety-three percent of all equities.

This single statistic explains more about monetary policy than any textbook.

When the Federal Reserve purchases bonds and mortgage-backed securities, it drives up asset prices. When asset prices rise, ninety-three percent of the benefit flows to the top ten percent. The remaining seven percent is shared among the other ninety percent of the population.

This is not a side effect of monetary policy. It is the transmission mechanism. Central banks cannot inject money into the economy evenly. They inject it through financial markets. Financial markets are owned overwhelmingly by the wealthy. Therefore, monetary expansion is, by mathematical necessity, a wealth transfer upward.

The stated purpose of quantitative easing is to "stimulate the economy." The measured effect is to enrich those who already own assets while eroding the purchasing power of those who do not.

A nurse who saves in cash loses purchasing power. A hedge fund manager whose portfolio inflates gains wealth. Both live under the same monetary policy. The outcomes are opposite.

This is not a conspiracy. It is arithmetic. The numbers are published by the Federal Reserve itself, in the Survey of Consumer Finances. Anyone can read them.

The design question: what would a monetary system look like if saving were not punished and asset ownership were not a prerequisite for preserving wealth?

## POST A7 - WHO MEASURES THE DOLLAR?

The dollar measures everything. The price of bread. The price of oil. The price of a house. The price of an hour of labour.

But who measures the dollar?

The answer is: the Federal Reserve. Through its control of money supply, interest rates, and inflation targets, the Federal Reserve determines the purchasing power of the instrument that measures all other things.

This creates a circular problem. The unit of measurement is defined by the entity that controls it, and that entity can redefine it at will. Imagine a ruler whose manufacturer could change the length of an inch whenever it suited them. Every measurement made with that ruler would be technically correct at the moment of measurement, and meaningless across time.

A dollar in 1971 and a dollar in 2026 carry the same name and the same printed numeral. They do not represent the same purchasing power. Not approximately. Not roughly. They differ by eighty-seven percent.

In my own research, I examined this problem through the lens of classical monetary analysis. A legitimate medium of exchange must be measurable by its own substance. Gold is measured by weight. A gram is a gram regardless of who holds it or when. Fiat currency is measured by declaration. The declaration is made by the authority that benefits from the measurement.

This is not a technical complaint. It is a foundational one. If the instrument that measures all economic value can be altered at will by a single institution, then no economic planning, no savings, no contract is truly stable.

The question: can a medium of exchange be legitimate if its unit of measurement is controlled by a single party?

## POST A8 - THE INTEREST IMPOSSIBILITY

A bank lends one million dollars at five percent interest. The borrower owes one million and fifty thousand. The bank created the one million when it issued the loan. It did not create the fifty thousand.

Where does the fifty thousand come from?

From someone else's loan. Someone else must borrow money into existence so that the first borrower can source the interest payment. That new loan carries its own interest. Which must be sourced from yet another loan.

This is not a simplification. This is the actual mechanism of fractional reserve banking. Money enters circulation through lending. Lending carries interest. The interest was never created. Therefore, the total obligations in the system permanently exceed the total money supply.

The implication is precise: in an interest-based monetary system, it is mathematically impossible for all debts to be repaid simultaneously. There will always be defaults. Not because borrowers are irresponsible, but because the money to repay all debts does not exist.

In my published research on the structural characteristics of exchange, I traced this problem to its first-principles root. The prohibition of interest in classical monetary scholarship was not merely moral. It was structural. The scholars who identified this problem over a thousand years ago understood the mathematics: if you charge interest on money you created, you have created an obligation that exceeds what exists. The system requires perpetual growth or periodic collapse.

We call those collapses "financial crises." They are not accidents. They are the mathematics working as designed.

## POST B3 - THE LOBBYING ASYMMETRY

The US financial sector spent seven hundred and twenty-one million dollars on lobbying in 2023.

The total spent by all consumer advocacy organisations combined is a fraction of that figure.

In the US system, lobbying is legal. It is documented. It is published by OpenSecrets.org for anyone to examine. What the numbers reveal is not corruption in the criminal sense. The Glass-Steagall Act, which separated commercial banking from investment banking, was repealed in 1999 after sustained lobbying by Citigroup and others. Sandy Weill, the CEO who led the lobbying effort, later publicly stated that repealing it was a mistake.

The Dodd-Frank Act of 2010 was designed to prevent another 2008. Its compliance costs were so high that small banks consolidated into large banks. The number of US banks has declined by over thirty percent since 2010. The regulation designed to curb concentration accelerated it.

This is a consistent pattern across regulated industries: the largest incumbents can absorb the cost of compliance. Smaller competitors cannot. Regulation, regardless of intent, tends to protect the established and exclude the emerging.

The question: is it possible to build a financial system whose rules cannot be lobbied? Where the specification is public, the parameters are fixed, and no amount of spending can alter the design?

The speech is public record, held at the Eisenhower Presidential Library. Sixty-five years later, it reads less like a warning and more like a blueprint.

## POST B5 - THE FIVE CAPTURED

Simon Dixon names five governments that he argues are captured by the military-industrial complex: the United States, the United Kingdom, Germany, France, and Israel. His criterion is simple: these governments print money and ensure it flows into defence spending, propping up the companies that profit from strategic tension.

The concept of regulatory capture is not new. George Stigler won the Nobel Prize in Economics in part for his 1971 paper, "The Theory of Economic Regulation," which demonstrated that regulatory agencies tend to serve the interests of the industries they regulate.

The mechanism is straightforward. The regulated industry has more expertise than the regulator. The regulator must hire from the industry to be competent. Former regulators move to lucrative industry positions. The implicit promise of future employment creates incentive alignment. The industry funds the campaigns of politicians who sit on oversight committees.

This is documented. It is legal. And it produces a consistent outcome: regulation that protects the incumbent and raises barriers for the newcomer.

The question Dixon raises is whether this pattern extends beyond individual agencies to entire governments, where military spending, financial policy, and technological infrastructure are coordinated not by conspiracy but by structural incentive alignment.

The answer is in the budgets. Follow the money. It is always in the budgets.

What if your money had an expiry date? What if it could only be spent on approved categories? What if it stopped working outside a specific postcode?

This is not speculative fiction. This is the documented capability of Central Bank Digital Currencies.

The question is not whether governments will use these capabilities. It is whether we will design alternative systems before the choice is made for us.

## POST C5 - TWO DESIGNS, SAME TECHNOLOGY

Blockchain can build surveillance. It can also build privacy. Digital identity can enable control. It can also protect rights. Cryptography can lock people out. It can also ensure that no one is locked out.

The technology is neutral. The design is everything.

A Central Bank Digital Currency designed for surveillance: the issuer sees every transaction, can freeze accounts without judicial process, can impose spending categories and expiry dates, can revoke access based on administrative decision.

A protocol designed for privacy: identity is verified once, transactions are pseudonymous, no authority can see what you buy, account access cannot be revoked except by court order, the rules are defined in the specification and cannot be altered by any individual or institution.

Both use the same underlying technology. Both use distributed ledgers. Both use cryptographic verification. Both use digital identity. The outcomes are opposite because the design intent is opposite.

One is designed for the issuer. The other is designed for the participant.

The question that will define the next decade of money is not "will money become digital?" It already has. The question is: who is the digital money designed to serve?

The answer is in the architecture, not the marketing.

## POST D1 - WHAT IF MONEY DID NOT LOSE VALUE?

Every central bank in the world has an explicit inflation target. Typically two percent per year. This is a choice. It is not a law of nature. It is not an inevitability. It is a policy decision made by a small number of people in a room.

The stated rationale: inflation encourages spending and investment. If your money loses value over time, you are incentivised to spend it or invest it rather than hold it.

The unstated consequence: savers are punished. Every person who sets aside a portion of their income for the future watches that future shrink, year after year, by policy decision.

What if the choice were different? What if money were designed with a fixed supply that no authority could expand? What if saving were not a losing proposition?

This is not a utopian fantasy. Gold functioned this way for millennia. Its supply grew slowly, limited by the physical difficulty of extraction. No emperor could print more gold. The saver who buried gold coins could dig them up a decade later and find their purchasing power intact.

The argument against fixed supply is that it prevents governments from responding to crises. The argument for it is that every crisis response funded by money creation transfers wealth from the many to the few, as we have documented in previous posts.

The question is not whether a fixed-supply medium is technically possible. It is. The question is whether we are willing to give up the ability to dilute everyone's savings in exchange for the stability of knowing that no one can.

## POST D2 - THE THREE PILLARS

During the golden age of scholarship, roughly the eighth through fourteenth centuries, the Islamic world made foundational contributions to economics that are rarely credited in Western curricula.

Ibn Khaldun developed cyclical theories of civilisation and early supply-demand analysis. Al-Ghazali articulated the three functions of money: medium of exchange, unit of account, and store of value. Abu Yusuf wrote on taxation and public finance.

From this body of work emerged three pillars of sound economics:

Free trade: voluntary exchange between willing parties, without coercion.
Hard money: a medium of exchange whose supply is constrained by its physical nature, typically gold and silver, not expandable at the will of a ruler.
Ethics: the concept that trade has a moral dimension, that some exchanges are harmful regardless of whether both parties consent.

Adam Smith adopted the first two. He replaced the third with the "invisible hand," a self-regulating market mechanism that treated ethical questions as irrelevant to economic efficiency.

Simon Dixon observes that Smith's face appeared on the twenty-pound note at the Bank of England, because his framework justified the worship of markets within a debt-based monetary system.

What was lost when ethics was removed from economics? The concept that a system which structurally guarantees that debt exceeds money supply is not "efficient." It is predatory.

The question: can economics function without an ethical dimension? Or does the removal of ethics from trade inevitably produce the extraction we see today?

## POST D3 - THE GOLD DILUTION

Consider a merchant in the ancient world. He takes one ounce of pure gold, melts it, and adds base metals to produce a heavier alloy that looks like gold but is not. He presents this diluted metal as equivalent to pure gold in an exchange.

This is fraud. It was recognised as fraud thousands of years ago. Classical scholarship had a precise term for it: adulteration of the medium.

Now consider a modern government. It issues a paper note denominated as one dollar. In 1913, that dollar had defined gold backing. Over the following century, the backing was progressively removed and finally abolished in 1971. By the Federal Reserve's own data, the dollar has lost over ninety-seven percent of its purchasing power since 1913.

The note carries the same name. The same printed numeral. The same legal status. But it does not represent the same wealth. The denomination remains. The substance continuously shrinks.

The mechanism is different from the ancient merchant's crucible. The economic reality is identical. More units, less substance.

In my research, I examined whether modern fiat currency meets the classical criteria for a legitimate medium of exchange. The findings were clear: fiat fails on fungibility across time, on measurability by its own substance, and on storability of value. The equivalence between a dollar today and a dollar a decade from now is decreed, not intrinsic.

The question: if we would prosecute the merchant for diluting gold, what do we call the institution that dilutes the currency?

## POST D4 - LENDING WITHOUT INTEREST

If I lend you capital and we share the profit, I am incentivised to help you succeed. Your success is my return. I will offer advice, connections, patience. Our interests are aligned.

If I charge you interest, I profit whether you succeed or fail. I am incentivised to lend, collect, and move on. If you default, I seize collateral. Your failure is an acceptable outcome because the terms are structured to protect me regardless.

These are not two versions of the same thing. They are two fundamentally different architectures of economic relationship.

Interest-based lending creates a mathematical impossibility, as we explored in a previous post. The interest owed was never created. Someone must default. The lender is protected. The borrower bears the structural risk.

Profit-sharing lending creates alignment. The lender evaluates not just the borrower's ability to repay, but the viability of the venture. Capital flows toward productive enterprise because the lender's return depends on actual outcomes.

This is not a theoretical distinction. Cooperative finance models based on profit-sharing have operated successfully in various forms for centuries. They are not mainstream because the interest-based system is more profitable for lenders, not because they are less effective for communities.

The question: which system produces healthier communities? The one where the lender profits from the borrower's obligation, or the one where both parties profit from the borrower's success?

## POST D5 - THE PRESERVATION QUESTION

The preservation of wealth is recognised as a fundamental objective across every major ethical, legal, and scholarly tradition in human history. The idea that what a person earns through their labour should retain its value is not controversial. It is foundational.

The modern fiat monetary system defeats this objective by design.

A two percent annual inflation target means that the purchasing power of savings is halved approximately every thirty-six years. A lifetime of work, saved diligently, loses half its value before the saver reaches retirement.

This erosion is not an accident. It is not a side effect. It is the published, defended, explicitly targeted policy of every major central bank.

In my research, I examined whether a medium of exchange that systematically erodes the wealth it holds can be considered legitimate under classical monetary criteria. The analysis was clear: a medium that fails to preserve value across time fails at one of the three fundamental functions of money, the store of value.

If the medium of exchange itself defeats the preservation of wealth, then every transaction conducted in that medium occurs on a foundation that is structurally unsound. The individual is placed in a position where the only way to preserve purchasing power is to take risk, invest, and accept counterparty exposure. Saving alone is not enough. The system will erode what you save.

The question: does the obligation to preserve wealth sit with the individual alone? Or does it extend to the design of the medium itself?

## POST D6 - WHO DESIGNED THIS FOR?

Every system was designed by someone, for someone.

The modern financial system was designed in an era of physical ledgers, physical vaults, and physical distance between transacting parties. Intermediaries were necessary because trust between strangers required a trusted third party. Banks, correspondent banks, exchanges, payment processors, clearinghouses: each exists because of a genuine historical need.

Those needs have largely been met by technology. Transactions can settle in seconds. Identity can be verified cryptographically. Ledgers can be distributed and auditable. The historical justification for most financial intermediaries has been dissolved by the technology of the last two decades.

Yet the intermediaries remain. And they charge the same fees they charged when their services were indispensable.

SWIFT processes five trillion dollars daily. Visa and Mastercard handle eighty percent of global card payments. Remittance companies extract forty-eight to fifty-three billion dollars annually from migrant workers. These fees are not the cost of technology. They are the cost of a system designed by and for intermediaries that no longer need to exist.

The question I return to, again and again: what would money look like if it were designed today, from scratch, for the person who has the least? Not for the bank. Not for the payment processor. Not for the fund manager. For the teacher. For the farmer. For the migrant worker sending money home.

That is not a rhetorical question. It is a design problem. And design problems have solutions.

## POST E2 - COUNTERPARTY RISK IS TIME THEFT

Simon Dixon makes an observation I have never heard articulated so precisely: every investment with counterparty risk steals your time.

Private equity: board meetings, legal disputes, rogue founders, compliance obligations. Public equities: monitoring, earnings calls, market anxiety. Angel investments: "Every single one of them let me down," Dixon says.

He contrasts this with self-custody assets. Bitcoin in his own wallet does not call him into a board meeting. Gold bars in his vault do not require quarterly reporting. They sit. They hold value. They consume none of his time.

Time is the only non-renewable resource. You cannot earn more of it. You cannot save it for later. You cannot inherit it. Every hour spent managing a counterparty relationship is an hour taken from your family, your health, your community, your thinking.

We measure investment returns in percentages. We rarely measure the time cost. If you calculated the true return on a private equity position as profits minus the monetary value of hours spent managing it, many investments have negative returns.

The principle extends beyond personal finance. Every intermediary in the financial system introduces time cost. Every counterparty relationship creates dependency. Every dependency consumes attention.

The design question: what would a financial system look like if it minimised counterparty risk to zero? If holding and using money required no intermediary, no manager, no board, no compliance officer?
