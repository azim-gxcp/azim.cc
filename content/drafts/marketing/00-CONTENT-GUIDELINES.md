## 1. What GX Protocol IS and IS NOT

| GX Protocol IS | GX Protocol IS NOT |
|----------------|--------------------|
| A protocol specification | A product or startup |
| A public utility | A commercial platform |
| Rule-based and constraint-driven | Discretionary or promise-based |
| Non-profit | An investment or speculative asset |
| Transparent and auditable | Marketing-driven |

**One-sentence description**: GX Protocol defines a rule-based, fixed-supply global medium of exchange distributed by grant to every verified participant, with zero inflation, zero interest on lending, and near-zero cross-border transaction costs.

## 3. Tone Rules

### Required Tone

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Precise** | Specific numbers, not vague claims. "0.15% cross-border fee" not "low fees" |
| **Neutral** | No promotional or emotionally persuasive language |
| **Public utility** | Frame as infrastructure, not a product to sell |
| **Non-profit** | Never imply commercial return or investment potential |
| **Inviting** | Tone should welcome scrutiny, not be adversarial |
| **Confident without being aggressive** | State facts, let the numbers speak |

### Absolutely Prohibited

- "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "disruptive," "next-generation"
- "Invest," "returns," "value appreciation," "to the moon"
- "Join the movement," "be part of the future," "don't miss out"
- "Exciting," "amazing," "incredible opportunity"
- Any religious terminology (Islamic finance, Sharia, halal, Takaful, Qard Hassan)
- Any framing of GX failure scenarios ("if GX fails...")
- Founder-centred language ("our vision," "we believe")
- Anthropomorphising ("GX Protocol wants," "the protocol believes")
- Attribution to AI or any specific technology brand

### Preferred Phrasing

| Instead of | Use |
|------------|-----|
| "GX Protocol aims to..." | "The GX Protocol defines..." |
| "Users can enjoy..." | "Participants operate under..." |
| "We launched a new feature" | "Protocol version X.Y activates..." |
| "Our platform offers..." | "The protocol specifies..." |
| "Nothing to lose" | "No financial risk" |
| "If GX fails..." | Do not use — focus on what participants can do to make it work |

## 4. Content Principles

### Lead with the Hardest Question
Open every piece of content by naming the strongest objection — before the audience raises it. This signals intellectual honesty and disarms reflexive skepticism.

### Quantify Everything
Abstract claims ("GX is fair," "GX is efficient") are dismissed. Specific numbers ("0.15% cross-border fee vs 3-7% traditional remittance") are evidence. Every claim must be specific and verifiable.

### Invite Scrutiny Directly
Point the audience to the published soundness analysis. Say explicitly: "We scored ourselves at 88%. We published our weaknesses. If you find more, we want to know."

### Never Discuss Failure Scenarios
Do not frame GX in negative scenarios. Focus on what every participant can do to make GX successful — upholding the purchasing power granted to them, participating actively in the ecosystem.

### No Religious References
Any resemblance to religious economic models is coincidental. GX was designed from modern technology and analysis of fiat system defects, not from any religious framework. Use "cooperative finance" or "profit-sharing finance" when referencing interest-free models.

## 5. Platform-Specific Guidelines

### LinkedIn
- Professional, evidence-heavy tone
- Lead with data and comparisons
- 1,500-2,250 characters for posts
- Use tables and bullet points
- End with a question to invite discussion
- Suitable content: economic analysis, comparisons, government value proposition, FAQ responses

### Instagram
- Visual-first: infographics, comparison charts, short text overlays
- Carousel posts for educational content (e.g., "5 things you didn't know about cross-border fees")
- Reels: 30-60 seconds, one concept per video
- Captions: concise, end with call to examine (not call to action)
- Suitable content: remittance savings, inflation comparisons, "myth vs fact"

### TikTok
- 15-60 second educational videos
- Hook in first 2 seconds ("Did you know you lose $14 every time you send $200 home?")
- One concept per video — no complexity
- Conversational tone (still precise, but accessible)
- Suitable content: remittance costs, lost Bitcoin, inflation data, "too good to be true" responses

### YouTube
- Longer-form educational content (5-15 minutes)
- Whiteboard explainers for velocity mechanism, grant distribution, comparisons
- Interview format for FAQ responses
- Suitable content: full soundness analysis walkthrough, comparative deep-dives

### X/Twitter
- Short, punchy, data-driven
- Thread format for multi-point arguments
- Single-stat tweets with context
- Suitable content: daily facts, comparison stats, invitation to scrutiny

## 7. Fact-Checking Requirement

Every number used in content must be traceable to one of these sources:
- GX Protocol specification documents (published)
- World Bank (remittance data)
- IMF (forex reserves, monetary data)
- BIS (transaction volumes)
- GSMA (mobile money data)
- National central bank reports (currency depreciation)
- Published company annual reports (Visa, Mastercard, Safaricom, etc.)

Do not use numbers without attribution. Do not round aggressively (USD 48B is acceptable for USD 48-53B; USD 50B without noting the range is not).
